================================================================================
  ENDOMETRIOSIS — DIAGNOSIS AND MANAGEMENT
================================================================================

Title   : Endometriosis — Diagnosis and Management
Source  : American Society for Reproductive Medicine (ASRM) Guidelines;
          European Society of Human Reproduction and Embryology (ESHRE);
          ACOG Practice Bulletin; NIH / NICHD; World Endometriosis Society
Disease : Endometriosis

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
OVERVIEW
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Endometriosis is a chronic, estrogen-dependent inflammatory disease defined
by the presence of endometrial-like tissue (glands and stroma) outside the
uterine cavity. It affects approximately 10% of reproductive-age women globally
(~190 million), with prevalence of 40–50% among women with infertility or
chronic pelvic pain. The most common sites are the ovaries (endometriomas),
peritoneum, uterosacral ligaments, pouch of Douglas, bowel, and bladder.
Pathogenesis is multifactorial and incompletely understood; theories include
retrograde menstruation (Sampson), coelomic metaplasia, lymphovascular
spread, and immune dysfunction allowing ectopic implantation and survival.
Endometriotic lesions are hormonally active and produce prostaglandins, cytokines,
and local estrogen via aromatase activity, driving inflammation, adhesion
formation, fibrosis, and pain. Average diagnostic delay is 7–10 years.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SYMPTOMS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Pain (Cardinal Symptoms):
  - Dysmenorrhea: Severe, progressive menstrual cramping; often begins days
    before period; does not respond adequately to NSAIDs
  - Chronic pelvic pain: Non-cyclical pelvic pain >6 months
  - Dyspareunia: Deep pain with sexual intercourse (especially posterior
    compartment disease)
  - Dyschezia: Painful defecation (bowel endometriosis)
  - Dysuria: Painful urination (bladder endometriosis)
  - Cyclical rectal bleeding/hematuria (bowel/bladder involvement)

Reproductive:
  - Infertility (40–50% of infertile women have endometriosis)
  - Recurrent pregnancy loss

Systemic Symptoms:
  - Fatigue (chronic inflammation)
  - Bloating, nausea, diarrhea (GI involvement)
  - Sciatica, leg pain (sciatic nerve involvement)
  - Shoulder/diaphragmatic pain (catamenial pneumothorax — rare thoracic disease)

Endometrioma-Specific:
  - Adnexal mass (ovarian endometrioma/"chocolate cyst")
  - Risk of cyst rupture with severe acute pain

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
DIAGNOSIS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Clinical Assessment:
  - Detailed menstrual/pain/sexual history
  - Physical examination: Fixed, tender uterus in retroversion, tender
    uterosacral nodularity on bimanual/speculum exam, adnexal mass

Imaging:
  Transvaginal Ultrasound (TVUS):
    - First-line imaging modality
    - Detects endometriomas (typical "ground glass" echogenicity, thick walls)
    - Limited sensitivity for peritoneal/superficial lesions
  MRI Pelvis:
    - Superior for deep infiltrating endometriosis (DIE)
    - Evaluates extent: Bowel, bladder, ureter, uterosacral ligament involvement
    - T1 hyperintense, T2 shading in endometriomas

Surgical Diagnosis (Definitive):
  - Laparoscopy with direct visualization and histological confirmation
  - Gold standard for diagnosis
  - American Society for Reproductive Medicine (ASRM/rASRM) Staging I–IV:
    Stage I (Minimal) → Stage IV (Severe; dense adhesions, endometriomas)
  - Lesion types: Superficial peritoneal, ovarian endometrioma, deep
    infiltrating endometriosis (DIE)

Laboratory:
  - CA-125: Elevated in moderate-severe disease; non-specific (also elevated
    in other conditions); limited diagnostic utility; used for monitoring
  - No reliable blood biomarker for diagnosis currently

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TREATMENT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

1. PAIN MANAGEMENT — FIRST-LINE MEDICAL

   NSAIDs (First-line for dysmenorrhea):
     - Naproxen 500 mg BID, ibuprofen 400–800 mg TID, mefenamic acid 500 mg TID
     - Taken continuously from 1–2 days before expected pain onset
     - Reduces prostaglandin-mediated dysmenorrhea; does not alter progression

   Combined Oral Contraceptives (COC):
     - Any monophasic low-dose OCP (ethinyl estradiol 20–35 mcg + progestin)
     - Continuous (no pill-free interval) or cyclic regimens
     - Suppresses ovulation; reduces menstrual flow and ectopic tissue stimulation
     - Effective for dysmenorrhea and pain; does not cure or prevent progression
     - Side effects: Nausea, breast tenderness, mood changes, VTE risk

2. PROGESTOGENS — Second-Line / First-Line for Endometrioma

   Norethindrone Acetate (NETA):
     - 5–15 mg/day orally
     - Induces decidualization/atrophy of endometriotic lesions
     - Effective for pain including DIE; long-term maintenance

   Medroxyprogesterone Acetate (MPA, Depo-Provera):
     - 150 mg IM every 3 months (injectable DMPA)
     - 10–50 mg/day orally
     - Highly effective for pain; reduces bleeding; associated with bone density
       loss and delayed return of fertility with DMPA

   Levonorgestrel IUS (Mirena):
     - 52 mg LNG-IUS intrauterine device
     - Reduces dysmenorrhea significantly; amenorrhea common
     - Local progestin delivery; minimal systemic effects
     - Effective for minimal-mild endometriosis and post-surgical maintenance

   Dienogest (Visanne):
     - 2 mg/day orally
     - Highly selective progestin; anti-inflammatory; reduces endometriotic
       tissue; comparable efficacy to GnRH agonist with fewer side effects
     - First-choice progestin in many European guidelines
     - Side effects: Irregular bleeding, spotting, mild bone loss

   Etonogestrel Implant (Nexplanon):
     - Subdermal 68 mg implant; effective pain reduction in some patients

3. GnRH AGONISTS — Second-Line
   Leuprolide Acetate (Lupron Depot):
     - 3.75 mg IM monthly or 11.25 mg every 3 months
   Nafarelin (Synarel): 200 mcg intranasal twice daily
   Goserelin (Zoladex): 3.6 mg SC monthly
   Buserelin, triptorelin

   Mechanism: Initial flare, then downregulation of GnRH receptor → hypogonadal
   state → profound estrogen suppression → endometriotic tissue atrophy
   Efficacy: Highly effective for pain; equivalent to surgery in studies
   Duration: Maximum 6 months (bone loss concern) without add-back
   Add-back Therapy (mandatory >6 months or earlier):
     - Norethindrone acetate 5 mg/day ± low-dose estrogen (0.625 mg CEE)
     - Protects bone mineral density; does not compromise efficacy for pain

   Side Effects (hypoestrogenic): Hot flashes, vaginal dryness, mood changes,
   insomnia, bone density loss (reversible with add-back)

4. GnRH ANTAGONISTS (Oral)
   Elagolix (Orilissa):
     - 150 mg once daily (moderate pain) or 200 mg twice daily (severe pain)
     - Immediate, dose-dependent estrogen suppression without flare
     - FDA-approved for moderate-to-severe endometriosis pain
     - 150 mg/day × 24 months; 200 mg BID × 6 months (bone loss limits duration)
     - Add-back therapy recommended for 200 mg BID regimen

   Relugolix-estradiol-norethindrone (Myfembree):
     - 40/1.0/0.5 mg once daily (relugolix 40 mg + E2 1 mg + NETA 0.5 mg)
     - Built-in add-back; FDA-approved for pain associated with endometriosis
     - Approved for 24 months

   Linzagolix (Yselty):
     - Approved in EU; 75 mg or 200 mg daily ± add-back

5. AROMATASE INHIBITORS (for Refractory Disease)
   Letrozole 2.5 mg/day or Anastrozole 1 mg/day:
     - Inhibits local estrogen synthesis in endometriotic lesions (aromatase P450)
     - Used in refractory cases not responding to standard hormonal therapy
     - Combined with progestin or OCP to suppress ovarian estrogen and prevent
       follicular cysts
     - Not first or second-line; research/specialist use

6. SURGICAL TREATMENT
   Laparoscopic Excision (Preferred):
     - Gold standard for diagnosis AND treatment
     - Excision > ablation for endometriomas and DIE (lower recurrence)
     - Ovarian cystectomy for endometriomas ≥3 cm (stripping technique)
     - Excision of peritoneal lesions; restoration of normal anatomy;
       adhesiolysis
     - Ureterolysis, bowel shaving/segmental resection (colorectal surgeons)
       for DIE involving ureters/bowel
     - Multidisciplinary approach essential for advanced DIE

   Laparotomy:
     - For complex DIE requiring bowel resection, hysterectomy

   Hysterectomy + Bilateral Salpingo-Oophorectomy (BSO):
     - Definitive surgery for severe, refractory, childbearing-complete cases
     - BSO reduces recurrence risk
     - Discuss HRT after surgical menopause (low-dose estrogen generally safe
       post-BSO for endometriosis; combined with progestin if uterus retained)

7. INFERTILITY MANAGEMENT
   - Laparoscopic treatment of endometriosis improves natural conception rates
     (especially Stage I/II — ENDO trial debate)
   - Endometrioma: Surgical cystectomy before IVF improves oocyte yield
     (debate: may reduce ovarian reserve; individualized decision)
   - Controlled Ovarian Stimulation + IUI: For minimal-mild disease
   - IVF: For moderate-severe disease or failed IUI; endometriosis reduces IVF
     success; GnRH agonist down-regulation preferred protocol
   - Avoid surgery <3 months before IVF (follicle pool recovery)

8. ADJUNCTIVE AND EMERGING THERAPIES
   - Pentoxifylline (anti-inflammatory/immunomodulatory): Limited evidence
   - Low-dose naltrexone: Immunomodulatory; investigational
   - Omega-3 fatty acids, anti-inflammatory diet: Symptom support
   - Pelvic floor physiotherapy for chronic pelvic pain and dyspareunia
   - Pain psychology and multidisciplinary pain management
   - TNF-α inhibitors, anti-angiogenics: Investigational
   - JAK inhibitors: Pre-clinical studies

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
MONITORING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  - Clinical review every 3–6 months during medical treatment
  - TVUS: Annual for endometrioma surveillance; pre-operatively for DIE extent
  - CA-125: Baseline and follow-up for moderate-severe disease (monitoring
    treatment response, not diagnosis)
  - Bone density (DEXA): Before/after GnRH agonist therapy >6 months
  - Post-surgical: Resumption of hormonal therapy to prevent recurrence
    (recurrence rate 40–50% at 5 years without suppression)
  - Fertility planning: Timely referral for reproductive endocrinology

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
COMPLICATIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  - Infertility (tubal, ovarian reserve damage, implantation failure)
  - Ovarian endometrioma rupture
  - Ureteral obstruction and hydronephrosis (DIE)
  - Bowel obstruction or perforation (bowel endometriosis)
  - Catamenial pneumothorax (thoracic endometriosis — rare)
  - Malignant transformation (endometriosis-associated ovarian cancer — rare;
    clear cell and endometrioid subtypes)
  - Depression, anxiety, impaired quality of life
