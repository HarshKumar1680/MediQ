

Title   : Celiac Disease — Diagnosis and Management
Source  : American College of Gastroenterology (ACG) Guidelines; NIH / NIDDK;
          Mayo Clinic; World Gastroenterology Organisation (WGO) Global Guidelines
Disease : Celiac Disease (Gluten-Sensitive Enteropathy)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
OVERVIEW
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Celiac disease is a chronic, immune-mediated enteropathy triggered by dietary
gluten (a protein found in wheat, barley, and rye) in genetically predisposed
individuals. It is characterized by villous atrophy of the small intestinal
mucosa, leading to malabsorption of nutrients. Approximately 1% of the global
population is affected, though the majority remain undiagnosed. The condition
is strongly associated with HLA-DQ2 and HLA-DQ8 haplotypes. Pathophysiology
involves both adaptive (T-cell mediated) and innate immune responses to gliadin
peptides, resulting in mucosal inflammation, crypt hyperplasia, and villous
blunting. Untreated celiac disease carries risks of osteoporosis, infertility,
lymphoma, and other autoimmune disorders.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SYMPTOMS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Classic GI Symptoms:
  - Chronic diarrhea (steatorrhea — pale, bulky, foul-smelling stools)
  - Abdominal bloating, distension, and cramping
  - Flatulence and nausea
  - Weight loss and failure to thrive (children)

Non-Classic / Extra-Intestinal Manifestations:
  - Iron-deficiency anemia (most common presentation in adults)
  - Dermatitis herpetiformis (intensely pruritic vesicular rash)
  - Peripheral neuropathy, ataxia
  - Osteopenia and osteoporosis
  - Infertility, recurrent miscarriage
  - Elevated liver transaminases (celiac hepatitis)
  - Aphthous ulcers, dental enamel defects
  - Fatigue, depression, cognitive impairment ("brain fog")
  - Short stature in children, delayed puberty

Silent/Asymptomatic Celiac Disease:
  - Positive serology and histology without symptoms
  - Often detected on screening of high-risk groups

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
DIAGNOSIS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Serological Testing (performed while on gluten-containing diet):
  - Anti-tissue transglutaminase IgA (tTG-IgA): First-line test; sensitivity
    >95%, specificity >95%
  - Anti-endomysial IgA (EMA-IgA): High specificity (>99%), used for
    confirmation
  - Deamidated gliadin peptide IgG/IgA (DGP): Useful in IgA-deficient patients
  - Total serum IgA: Must be measured to exclude selective IgA deficiency
    (~2-3% of celiac patients)

Endoscopic Findings:
  - Upper GI endoscopy with multiple duodenal biopsies (≥4 from D2 and ≥1 from
    duodenal bulb D1)
  - Scalloping of duodenal folds, mosaic pattern, loss of folds

Histological Classification (Modified Marsh Criteria):
  - Marsh 0: Normal mucosa
  - Marsh 1: Increased intraepithelial lymphocytes (IELs >25/100 enterocytes)
  - Marsh 2: Crypt hyperplasia + increased IELs
  - Marsh 3a: Partial villous atrophy
  - Marsh 3b: Subtotal villous atrophy
  - Marsh 3c: Total villous atrophy (classic celiac)

Genetic Testing:
  - HLA-DQ2/DQ8 typing: High negative predictive value; used to exclude disease
    in ambiguous cases

Diagnosis Confirmation:
  - Positive serology + Marsh ≥2 histology on gluten-containing diet, with
    clinical/histological response to gluten-free diet (GFD)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TREATMENT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

1. GLUTEN-FREE DIET (GFD) — THE ONLY PROVEN TREATMENT
   - Strict, lifelong elimination of wheat, barley, rye, and contaminated oats
   - Allowed: rice, maize, potato, quinoa, certified gluten-free oats
   - Dietary counseling by a registered dietitian is essential
   - Monitoring for nutritional deficiencies: iron, folate, B12, calcium,
     vitamin D, zinc, magnesium
   - Label-reading education; cross-contamination avoidance
   - Threshold for harm: <10–50 mg/day of gluten

2. NUTRITIONAL SUPPLEMENTATION
   Iron:
     - Ferrous sulfate 325 mg orally once–three times daily
     - IV iron (ferric carboxymaltose) for severe deficiency or intolerance
   Folate:
     - Folic acid 1 mg/day orally
   Vitamin B12:
     - Cyanocobalamin 1000 mcg IM monthly or 1000–2000 mcg orally daily
   Calcium + Vitamin D:
     - Calcium carbonate 1000–1500 mg/day
     - Vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol) 1000–2000 IU/day; adjust per serum levels
   Zinc:
     - Zinc sulfate 220 mg orally daily if deficient

3. REFRACTORY CELIAC DISEASE (RCD)
   Definition: Persistent/recurrent symptoms and villous atrophy despite strict
   GFD for >12 months after excluding dietary gluten exposure and other causes.

   RCD Type I (normal IEL phenotype):
     - Nutritional support
     - Budesonide 9 mg/day orally for 8–12 weeks (tapering)
     - Prednisolone 20–40 mg/day if budesonide unavailable
     - Azathioprine 2–2.5 mg/kg/day as steroid-sparing agent

   RCD Type II (clonal/aberrant IEL phenotype — pre-lymphomatous):
     - Cladribine (2-chlorodeoxyadenosine) 0.1 mg/kg/day SC for 5 days,
       cycles repeated (disease-modifying)
     - Autologous hematopoietic stem cell transplantation (HSCT) in eligible
       patients
     - Investigational: Anti-IL-15 therapy (AMG-714)

4. DERMATITIS HERPETIFORMIS
   - Strict GFD (skin lesions may take 6–24 months to resolve)
   - Dapsone 25–100 mg/day orally for rapid symptom control
     (monitor for hemolysis and methemoglobinemia)

5. EMERGING / INVESTIGATIONAL THERAPIES
   - Latiglutenase (ALV003): Oral glutenase enzyme to degrade ingested gluten
   - Nexvax2: Peptide-based immunotherapy (Phase II trials)
   - Anti-IL-15 monoclonal antibodies
   - Tight junction modulators (larazotide acetate)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
MONITORING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  - Serological follow-up: tTG-IgA at 6 and 12 months after GFD initiation,
    then annually
  - Repeat duodenal biopsy at 12–24 months in symptomatic or serology-positive
    patients
  - Bone mineral density (DEXA scan) at diagnosis; repeat per osteoporosis
    guidelines
  - Annual CBC, iron studies, B12, folate, vitamin D, liver enzymes
  - Thyroid function (associated autoimmune thyroid disease)
  - Annual dietary review with dietitian

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PREVENTION / COMPLICATIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Complications of Untreated Celiac Disease:
  - Osteoporosis and fractures
  - Iron-deficiency and megaloblastic anemia
  - Enteropathy-associated T-cell lymphoma (EATL) — rare but serious
  - Small bowel adenocarcinoma
  - Hyposplenism and increased infection risk
  - Infertility, adverse pregnancy outcomes

Associated Autoimmune Conditions:
  - Type 1 diabetes mellitus (~5–10% co-occurrence)
  - Autoimmune thyroid disease (Hashimoto's, Graves')
  - Primary biliary cholangitis
  - IgA nephropathy

Screening Recommendations:
  - First-degree relatives of confirmed celiac patients (15–20% prevalence)
  - Patients with type 1 diabetes, Down syndrome, Turner syndrome, William
    syndrome, IgA deficiency, autoimmune thyroid disease
