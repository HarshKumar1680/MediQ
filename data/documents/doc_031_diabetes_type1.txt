Title: Type 1 Diabetes - Insulin Therapy and Management
Source: Based on Wikipedia, Mayo Clinic, ADA Standards of Care
Disease: Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus

Type 1 diabetes mellitus (T1DM) is an autoimmune disease in which the immune system destroys the insulin-producing beta cells of the pancreatic islets of Langerhans. Without insulin, the body cannot use glucose for energy, leading to hyperglycemia, diabetic ketoacidosis, and, untreated, death.

OVERVIEW
T1DM typically presents in childhood or young adulthood but can occur at any age. Patients are absolutely dependent on exogenous insulin for survival. Unlike Type 2 diabetes, T1DM is not caused by lifestyle factors and cannot be managed with oral agents alone.

INSULIN THERAPY
Insulin replacement is the cornerstone of T1DM management. The goal is to mimic normal physiological insulin secretion.

Basal-Bolus Insulin Regimen:
Basal insulin provides background insulin to control glucose overnight and between meals.
Long-acting insulin analogs: insulin glargine (U-100, U-300), insulin detemir, and insulin degludec (U-100, U-200). These provide smooth, near-peakless coverage lasting 24 hours or more.
Bolus (prandial) insulin is given before meals to manage postprandial glucose excursions.
Rapid-acting insulin analogs: insulin lispro, insulin aspart, insulin glulisine, faster aspart (Fiasp), and ultra-rapid lispro (URLi). These have a rapid onset (5-15 minutes) and short duration (3-4 hours), closely mimicking normal mealtime insulin secretion.
Correction doses of rapid-acting insulin address hyperglycemia between meals.

Intermediate-Acting Insulin:
NPH (neutral protamine Hagedorn) insulin has an intermediate duration and is used in twice-daily regimens, though less favored than analog-based basal-bolus regimens.

Premixed Insulin:
Fixed-ratio combinations of intermediate and rapid-acting insulins, less flexible for tight glycemic control.

Insulin Delivery Devices:
Insulin pens (disposable or reusable) are most widely used for multi-injection therapy.
Insulin pumps (continuous subcutaneous insulin infusion, CSII) deliver programmable basal rates and bolus doses, offering superior glycemic control and flexibility for appropriate patients.

Advanced Technology:
Continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) systems (Dexterity G7, Libre 3) provide real-time glucose data, trend arrows, and alarms, reducing hypoglycemia and improving HbA1c.
Hybrid closed-loop systems (artificial pancreas) automatically adjust insulin delivery based on CGM readings, approaching physiological insulin regulation. Examples include MiniMed 780G, Omnipod 5, and Tandem Control-IQ.

DIABETIC KETOACIDOSIS (DKA) MANAGEMENT
DKA is a life-threatening complication. Treatment includes IV fluid resuscitation (normal saline), IV insulin infusion, potassium replacement, and monitoring and treating the precipitating cause. Bicarbonate is rarely required.

HYPOGLYCEMIA MANAGEMENT
Mild-moderate: 15-20 g fast-acting carbohydrates (glucose tablets, juice), repeat if needed (15-15 rule).
Severe (unable to self-treat): intramuscular or intranasal glucagon. IV dextrose in healthcare settings.

IMMUNOTHERAPY RESEARCH
Teplizumab (anti-CD3 monoclonal antibody) delays clinical onset of T1DM by approximately 2 years in high-risk individuals with Stage 2 (presymptomatic) T1DM and is FDA-approved for this indication.

TRANSPLANTATION
Pancreas transplantation and islet cell transplantation can restore endogenous insulin secretion and eliminate hypoglycemia. Pancreas transplantation is typically performed simultaneously with kidney transplantation in patients with T1DM and end-stage renal disease.
