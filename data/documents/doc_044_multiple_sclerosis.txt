

Title   : Multiple Sclerosis (MS) — Diagnosis and Management
Source  : European Committee for Treatment and Research in Multiple Sclerosis
          (ECTRIMS); American Academy of Neurology (AAN); MS Society;
          NIH / NINDS; McDonald Criteria 2017
Disease : Multiple Sclerosis (MS)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
OVERVIEW
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Multiple sclerosis (MS) is a chronic, immune-mediated demyelinating disease of
the central nervous system (CNS) characterized by inflammation, demyelination,
axonal injury, and gliosis. It is the leading cause of non-traumatic disability
in young adults. Global prevalence is approximately 2.8 million, with highest
rates in Northern Europe, North America, and Australia. Peak onset is between
ages 20–40; female:male ratio 3:1. Pathogenesis involves autoreactive CD4+
T cells (Th1, Th17), CD8+ T cells, and B cells breaching the blood-brain
barrier, targeting myelin antigens (MBP, MOG, PLP), causing demyelinating
plaques in the white and gray matter. Genetic risk involves HLA-DRB1*15:01;
environmental factors include low vitamin D, EBV infection, smoking, and
childhood obesity. Disease course types: Relapsing-Remitting MS (RRMS; 85%),
Secondary Progressive MS (SPMS), Primary Progressive MS (PPMS), and
Clinically Isolated Syndrome (CIS).

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SYMPTOMS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Motor:
  - Limb weakness, spasticity, gait disturbance
  - Pyramidal signs: hyperreflexia, upgoing plantars

Sensory:
  - Paresthesias (numbness, tingling) in limbs and trunk
  - Lhermitte's sign: electric shock sensation on neck flexion (cervical cord)
  - Pain (neuropathic and musculoskeletal)

Visual:
  - Optic neuritis: unilateral painful vision loss; RAPD; classic presentation
  - Diplopia (internuclear ophthalmoplegia from MLF lesion)
  - Oscillopsia, nystagmus

Cerebellar/Brainstem:
  - Ataxia, intention tremor, dysmetria (Charcot's triad)
  - Dysarthria, dysphagia

Autonomic and Bladder/Bowel:
  - Neurogenic bladder (urgency, frequency, retention)
  - Constipation or fecal urgency
  - Sexual dysfunction

Cognitive and Psychiatric:
  - Cognitive impairment (processing speed, memory)
  - Depression (~50%), pseudobulbar affect
  - Fatigue (most common and disabling symptom, 90%)
  - Heat sensitivity (Uhthoff's phenomenon): temporary worsening with heat

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
DIAGNOSIS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
McDonald Criteria 2017 — Based on:
  1. Dissemination in Space (DIS): ≥1 T2 lesion in ≥2 CNS regions (periventricular,
     cortical/juxtacortical, infratentorial, spinal cord)
  2. Dissemination in Time (DIT): New T2 or Gd-enhancing lesion on follow-up MRI,
     OR simultaneous presence of Gd-enhancing and non-enhancing lesions

MRI Brain and Spine (Gadolinium-Enhanced):
  - Periventricular plaques (Dawson's fingers — perivenular orientation)
  - Juxtacortical, cortical, infratentorial, and spinal cord lesions
  - Active lesions: Gadolinium-enhancing (acute inflammation, BBB disruption)
  - T1 "black holes" (irreversible axonal loss)
  - Progressive brain atrophy

CSF Analysis:
  - Oligoclonal bands (OCBs): Present in >90% of MS patients (IgG bands
    in CSF but not serum — intrathecal immunoglobulin synthesis)
  - IgG index elevated (>0.7)
  - Mild pleocytosis (lymphocytes <50 cells/µL)
  - No CSF OCBs: Diagnose with caution; consider NMOSD

Evoked Potentials:
  - Visual evoked potentials (VEP): Prolonged P100 latency in optic neuritis
  - Somatosensory and brainstem auditory evoked potentials

Blood Tests (to exclude mimics):
  - AQP4-IgG and MOG-IgG (to differentiate NMOSD and MOGAD)
  - ANA, anti-dsDNA, ACE (sarcoidosis, SLE)
  - Vitamin B12, CBC, ESR, TSH

Disability Assessment:
  - Expanded Disability Status Scale (EDSS): 0 (normal) to 10 (death from MS)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TREATMENT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

1. ACUTE RELAPSE TREATMENT
   Methylprednisolone IV:
     - Mechanism: Reduces inflammation and accelerates myelin repair
     - Dose: 1000 mg/day IV for 3–5 days (no taper required)
     - Shortens relapse duration; does not alter long-term disability
   Oral methylprednisolone 500–1000 mg/day × 5 days: Non-inferior to IV
   Plasma Exchange (PLEX):
     - 5–7 exchanges over 14 days
     - For severe steroid-refractory relapses (transverse myelitis, severe
       optic neuritis)

2. DISEASE-MODIFYING THERAPIES (DMTs) — RRMS/CIS

   PLATFORM / FIRST-LINE THERAPIES (Moderate Efficacy):

   Beta-Interferons:
     - Interferon beta-1a (Avonex): 30 mcg IM weekly
     - Interferon beta-1a (Rebif): 22–44 mcg SC three times weekly
     - Interferon beta-1b (Betaseron/Extavia): 250 mcg SC every other day
     - Peginterferon beta-1a (Plegridy): 125 mcg SC every 2 weeks
     - Mechanism: Immunomodulatory; shifts cytokine balance, reduces T cell
       trafficking across BBB
     - Reduce relapse rate ~30–35%
     - Monitoring: LFTs, CBC, thyroid function; flu-like injection site reactions

   Glatiramer Acetate (Copaxone, Glatopa):
     - 20 mg SC daily or 40 mg SC three times weekly
     - Mechanism: Synthetic peptide; mimics MBP; induces T regulatory cells
     - Relapse reduction ~30%
     - Minimal systemic side effects; injection site reactions

   Dimethyl Fumarate (Tecfidera):
     - 240 mg orally twice daily (start 120 mg BID × 7 days)
     - Mechanism: Nrf2 pathway activation (antioxidant); lymphocyte depletion
     - Relapse reduction ~49%
     - Side effects: Flushing (take with food/aspirin), GI symptoms,
       lymphopenia — monitor lymphocyte count

   Diroximel Fumarate (Vumerity):
     - 231 mg orally twice daily; improved GI tolerability vs. DMF

   Teriflunomide (Aubagio):
     - 7 or 14 mg orally once daily
     - Mechanism: Inhibits dihydroorotate dehydrogenase (DHODH); pyrimidine
       synthesis inhibition; reduces lymphocyte proliferation
     - Relapse reduction ~32–36%
     - Monitoring: LFTs, CBC, BP; teratogenic — elimination procedure required

   Monomethyl fumarate (Bafiertam): 190 mg BID

   HIGH-EFFICACY THERAPIES (for active disease or treat-to-target):

   Natalizumab (Tysabri):
     - 300 mg IV every 4 weeks
     - Mechanism: Anti-α4-integrin monoclonal antibody; prevents lymphocyte
       trafficking into CNS by blocking VLA-4/VCAM-1 interaction
     - Relapse reduction ~68%
     - Risk: Progressive Multifocal Leukoencephalopathy (PML) caused by JC
       virus reactivation; risk stratification by anti-JCV antibody index
     - Monthly infusion; MRI monitoring for PML

   Fingolimod (Gilenya):
     - 0.5 mg orally once daily
     - Mechanism: Sphingosine-1-phosphate (S1P) receptor modulator;
       sequesters lymphocytes in lymph nodes
     - Relapse reduction ~55%
     - Monitoring: First-dose ECG/cardiac observation (bradycardia), LFTs,
       ophthalmology (macular edema), skin (squamous cell carcinoma)
     - Live vaccine contraindicated; varicella immunity required

   Siponimod (Mayzent): S1P receptor modulator; 2 mg/day; for RRMS and SPMS
   Ozanimod (Zeposia): 0.92 mg/day; S1P modulator
   Ponesimod (Ponvory): 20 mg/day

   Cladribine (Mavenclad):
     - 3.5 mg/kg total dose over 2 years (short oral courses)
     - Mechanism: Purine analog; selectively depletes lymphocytes
     - Relapse reduction ~58%
     - Monitoring: Lymphocyte count, liver function; teratogenic × 6 months

   Alemtuzumab (Lemtrada):
     - 12 mg/day IV × 5 days (Year 1), then × 3 days (Year 2)
     - Mechanism: Anti-CD52 antibody; depletes T and B lymphocytes; allows
       immune reconstitution
     - Relapse reduction ~49–69% vs. interferon
     - Secondary autoimmunity risk (thyroid disease, ITP, nephropathy) in 30–40%
     - Monthly monitoring for 48 months after last course

   Ocrelizumab (Ocrevus):
     - 300 mg IV × 2 doses (2 weeks apart), then 600 mg IV every 6 months
     - Mechanism: Humanized anti-CD20 antibody; depletes B cells
     - RRMS: Relapse reduction ~46–47% vs. interferon
     - PPMS: Reduces disability progression (first approved therapy for PPMS)
     - Risk: Infusion reactions, infections, hepatitis B reactivation, PML

   Ofatumumab (Kesimpta):
     - 20 mg SC monthly (after loading doses)
     - Anti-CD20; fully human antibody; self-administered
     - Similar efficacy to ocrelizumab for RRMS

   Ublituximab (Briumvi): 150 mg IV, then 450 mg IV every 24 weeks; anti-CD20

3. PRIMARY PROGRESSIVE MS (PPMS)
   - Ocrelizumab 600 mg IV every 6 months: Only approved DMT for PPMS
   - Reduces disability progression by ~25% vs. placebo

4. SECONDARY PROGRESSIVE MS (SPMS)
   - Siponimod: Reduces disability progression in active SPMS
   - Ofatumumab, cladribine: Evidence in active SPMS
   - Mitoxantrone: 12 mg/m² IV every 3 months (lifetime cumulative limit due
     to cardiotoxicity); for rapidly worsening SPMS

5. SYMPTOM MANAGEMENT
   Spasticity:
     - Baclofen 10–80 mg/day orally (GABA-B agonist)
     - Tizanidine 4–36 mg/day orally (alpha-2 agonist)
     - Intrathecal baclofen pump for severe refractory spasticity
     - Nabiximols (Sativex): THC/CBD oromucosal spray for MS spasticity

   Fatigue:
     - Amantadine 200 mg/day
     - Modafinil 100–200 mg/day
     - Energy conservation, sleep hygiene

   Neuropathic Pain:
     - Gabapentin 300–3600 mg/day
     - Pregabalin 150–600 mg/day
     - Amitriptyline 10–75 mg at night
     - Duloxetine 30–120 mg/day

   Bladder Dysfunction:
     - Overactive bladder: Oxybutynin 5–15 mg/day, solifenacin 5–10 mg/day
     - Clean intermittent catheterization (CIC) for retention
     - Desmopressin for nocturia

   Depression: SSRIs (sertraline 50–200 mg/day), SNRIs
   Cognitive Rehabilitation: Cognitive training, neuropsychological support
   Tremor: Propranolol, clonazepam, deep brain stimulation (DBS) for severe
   Walking Difficulty: Dalfampridine (4-aminopyridine) 10 mg orally twice
     daily — potassium channel blocker; improves walking speed in ~35%

6. REHABILITATION
   - Physiotherapy, occupational therapy, speech therapy
   - Exercise (aerobic and resistance training) — safe and beneficial in MS
   - Fatigue management programs, multidisciplinary MS teams

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
MONITORING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  - MRI brain (and spine if indicated) at 3–6 months after DMT start,
    then annually (or per agent-specific protocols)
  - EDSS assessment every 6 months
  - Regular CBC, LFTs, lymphocyte counts (agent-specific)
  - JCV antibody monitoring for natalizumab patients every 6 months
  - Ophthalmology for fingolimod/siponimod
  - Immunoglobulin levels for prolonged anti-CD20 therapy
  - Thyroid/renal/hematologic monitoring for alemtuzumab × 4 years

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
COMPLICATIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  - Progressive disability and wheelchair dependence
  - Recurrent UTIs, pressure ulcers (immobility)
  - Depression, suicidal ideation
  - Dysphagia and aspiration pneumonia (progressive disease)
  - PML (natalizumab, fingolimod, dimethyl fumarate)
  - Secondary autoimmunity (alemtuzumab)
