Title: Liver Cirrhosis - Treatment and Hepatological Management
Source: Based on Wikipedia, Mayo Clinic, EASL Guidelines
Disease: Liver Cirrhosis

Liver cirrhosis is a late-stage form of hepatic fibrosis resulting from long-term liver damage. It is characterized by replacement of normal liver tissue by diffuse fibrosis, regenerative nodules, and vascular remodeling, leading to portal hypertension and liver dysfunction.

OVERVIEW
Common causes include chronic alcohol use (alcoholic cirrhosis), chronic viral hepatitis B and C, non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD/NASH), autoimmune hepatitis, primary biliary cholangitis, primary sclerosing cholangitis, hereditary hemochromatosis, Wilson disease, and alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency.

CLASSIFICATION
Compensated cirrhosis: No overt complications, relatively preserved liver function.
Decompensated cirrhosis: Development of variceal bleeding, ascites, hepatic encephalopathy, or jaundice, indicating significant liver dysfunction and portal hypertension.

TREATMENT APPROACHES

Treating Underlying Causes:
Alcohol abstinence: Cessation of alcohol consumption is the most important intervention in alcoholic cirrhosis and can partially reverse fibrosis.
Antiviral therapy: Treating hepatitis B and C viruses eliminates the primary driver of inflammation, stabilizes fibrosis, and can lead to regression.
Weight loss and metabolic control: For NAFLD/NASH, lifestyle modification targeting weight loss of 7 to 10 percent significantly reduces liver inflammation and fibrosis.
Immunosuppression: Corticosteroids and azathioprine for autoimmune hepatitis.
Ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA) slows progression of primary biliary cholangitis.
Copper chelation (penicillamine, trientine) or zinc supplementation for Wilson disease.
Phlebotomy for hereditary hemochromatosis.

Portal Hypertension and Its Complications:
Varices and Variceal Bleeding:
Non-selective beta-blockers (propranolol, carvedilol) reduce portal pressure and prevent first variceal bleeding and rebleeding. Endoscopic band ligation eradicates esophageal varices.
Acute variceal bleeding: vasoconstrictors (terlipressin, somatostatin analogs: octreotide, vapreotide), emergency endoscopic band ligation or sclerotherapy, IV antibiotics (ceftriaxone), and balloon tamponade as bridge to definitive therapy. Transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunt (TIPS) is used for refractory bleeding.

Ascites:
Sodium restriction (88 mmol/day) and diuretics: spironolactone (aldosterone antagonist) and furosemide (loop diuretic) in 100:40 mg ratio are first-line treatment.
Refractory ascites: Large volume paracentesis with albumin infusion. TIPS reduces ascites recurrence.
Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (SBP): Intravenous cefotaxime or ceftriaxone, IV albumin infusion to prevent hepatorenal syndrome, long-term norfloxacin or trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole prophylaxis.

Hepatic Encephalopathy:
Lactulose is the first-line treatment, reducing ammonia production and absorption by acidifying colonic contents and acting as a laxative.
Rifaximin is a non-absorbable antibiotic that reduces gut bacterial ammonia production and prevents recurrent episodes.
Identifying and treating precipitating factors (infection, GI bleeding, electrolyte disturbances, constipation).
Dietary protein restriction is no longer recommended; adequate protein intake supports muscle mass preservation.

Hepatorenal Syndrome (HRS):
Vasoconstrictors: terlipressin is the most effective agent. Norepinephrine with albumin as alternative. Midodrine plus octreotide plus albumin may be used where terlipressin is unavailable.

Hepatocellular Carcinoma Surveillance:
Ultrasound with or without alpha-fetoprotein every 6 months for HCC screening in all patients with cirrhosis.

Liver Transplantation:
The only definitive treatment for end-stage liver disease. MELD score (Model for End-stage Liver Disease) based on bilirubin, creatinine, and INR guides organ allocation. Contraindications include active alcohol/substance use, extrahepatic malignancy, and severe cardiopulmonary disease.
