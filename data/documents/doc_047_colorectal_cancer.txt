

Title   : Colorectal Cancer (CRC) — Diagnosis and Management
Source  : American Cancer Society (ACS); NCCN Clinical Practice Guidelines;
          European Society for Medical Oncology (ESMO); American Society of
          Clinical Oncology (ASCO); NIH / NCI
Disease : Colorectal Cancer (Adenocarcinoma of the Colon and Rectum)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
OVERVIEW
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Colorectal cancer (CRC) is the third most common malignancy and second leading
cause of cancer-related death worldwide (~2 million new cases; ~935,000 deaths
annually). It arises predominantly as adenocarcinoma from colonic and rectal
epithelium. Pathogenesis involves stepwise accumulation of genetic mutations
along adenoma-carcinoma sequence: APC → KRAS/NRAS → TP53, or via serrated
(BRAF, CIMP) or mismatch repair deficient (MMR-d/MSI-H) pathways. Risk factors
include age >50, family history, Lynch syndrome (MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2),
familial adenomatous polyposis (FAP/APC), inflammatory bowel disease, obesity,
red/processed meat consumption, alcohol, tobacco, and physical inactivity.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SYMPTOMS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  - Rectal bleeding or blood in stool (bright red or dark/tarry)
  - Change in bowel habits: diarrhea, constipation, narrow stools
  - Abdominal pain, cramping, bloating
  - Feeling of incomplete bowel emptying (tenesmus — rectal cancer)
  - Iron-deficiency anemia (right-sided colon cancer)
  - Unexplained weight loss, fatigue
  - Palpable abdominal mass
  - Obstruction: acute severe abdominal pain, distension, vomiting (emergency)
  - Rectal mass on DRE

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
DIAGNOSIS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Colonoscopy (Gold Standard):
  - Full visualization of entire colon; polypectomy for polyps
  - Four-quadrant biopsies; tattooing of lesion site

Flexible Sigmoidoscopy:
  - Views to splenic flexure; misses right-sided lesions

CT Colonography (Virtual Colonoscopy):
  - Alternative for incomplete or refused optical colonoscopy

Fecal Tests (Screening):
  - Fecal immunochemical test (FIT): Annual; detects hemoglobin
  - FIT-DNA (Cologuard): Stool DNA + FIT every 3 years
  - Guaiac fecal occult blood test (gFOBT): Annual

Staging — CT Chest/Abdomen/Pelvis (with contrast):
  - Local extent, regional lymph nodes, hepatic/pulmonary metastases

MRI Pelvis (Rectal Cancer):
  - CRM (circumferential resection margin), mesorectal fascia, T/N staging
  - Essential for neoadjuvant chemoradiation planning

PET-CT:
  - For equivocal metastatic disease or suspected recurrence

Biomarkers:
  - CEA (carcinoembryonic antigen): Baseline staging and surveillance;
    not diagnostic
  - KRAS, NRAS, BRAF mutations: Predictive for anti-EGFR therapy eligibility
  - MMR/MSI status (IHC + PCR): Predictive for immunotherapy; Lynch syndrome
  - HER2 amplification: Emerging target (trastuzumab + pertuzumab for HER2+)
  - NTRK fusion: Larotrectinib/entrectinib indication

TNM Staging (AJCC 8th):
  - Stage I: T1–T2, N0, M0 (5-yr OS ~90%)
  - Stage II: T3–T4, N0, M0 (5-yr OS ~70–85%)
  - Stage III: Any T, N1–N2, M0 (5-yr OS ~40–70%)
  - Stage IV: Any T, Any N, M1 (5-yr OS ~14%)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TREATMENT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

1. SURGERY — PRIMARY TREATMENT FOR LOCALIZED CRC

   Colon Cancer:
     - Right hemicolectomy: Cecum, ascending colon, hepatic flexure tumors
     - Extended right hemicolectomy: Transverse colon
     - Left hemicolectomy: Descending colon
     - Sigmoid colectomy: Sigmoid colon
     - Laparoscopic/robotic approaches: Preferred for equivalent oncologic
       outcomes with faster recovery
     - Complete mesocolic excision (CME): Analogous to TME; improves nodal yield

   Rectal Cancer:
     - Total Mesorectal Excision (TME): Standard; excision of tumor with intact
       mesorectal envelope
     - Low anterior resection (LAR): Upper and mid-rectum; sphincter-preserving
     - Abdominoperineal resection (APR): Very low rectal tumors; permanent
       colostomy
     - Transanal TME (TaTME): Improved access for narrow pelvis/obesity
     - Local excision (TEM/TAMIS): Select T1N0 rectal cancers

   Stoma: Temporary or permanent colostomy/ileostomy as needed

   Liver Metastases:
     - Hepatic resection for resectable liver-only metastases (20–30% of Stage IV):
       Curative intent; 5-year OS 25–40%
     - Ablation: Radiofrequency ablation (RFA), microwave ablation (MWA)
     - Staged resections, portal vein embolization (PVE) to increase future
       liver remnant

2. NEOADJUVANT THERAPY (Rectal Cancer — Stage II/III)
   Standard Chemoradiation (CRT):
     - Long-course: 45–50.4 Gy EBRT + concurrent fluoropyrimidine
       (capecitabine 825 mg/m² BID or 5-FU CI) × 5–6 weeks
     - Surgery 8–12 weeks after completion
     - Short-course RT: 5 × 5 Gy, followed by chemotherapy then surgery
       (Total Neoadjuvant Therapy — TNT approach)

   Total Neoadjuvant Therapy (TNT):
     - Induction chemotherapy (FOLFIRINOX or CAPOX) + CRT → surgery
     - Higher pathological complete response (pCR) rates (~25%)
     - pCR patients may consider "watch and wait" (non-operative management)

3. ADJUVANT CHEMOTHERAPY

   Stage III Colon Cancer (Standard):
   FOLFOX (Oxaliplatin-Based — Most Common):
     - Oxaliplatin 85 mg/m² IV (Day 1) + Leucovorin 400 mg/m² IV +
       5-Fluorouracil (5-FU) 400 mg/m² IV bolus, then 2400 mg/m² CI × 46 hours
     - Every 2 weeks × 12 cycles (6 months)
     - Mechanism: Oxaliplatin — platinum alkylating agent; 5-FU — thymidylate
       synthase inhibitor (inhibits DNA synthesis)

   CAPOX (Capecitabine + Oxaliplatin):
     - Oxaliplatin 130 mg/m² IV (Day 1) + Capecitabine 1000 mg/m² BID
       (Days 1–14) every 3 weeks × 8 cycles (6 months)
     - Capecitabine: Oral prodrug of 5-FU; converted to 5-FU in tumor
     - 3-month CAPOX = 6-month efficacy for low-risk Stage III

   5-FU/LV (Fluorouracil + Leucovorin):
     - FLOX regimen; alternative when oxaliplatin contraindicated
     - Older patients or peripheral neuropathy risk

   Stage II Colon Cancer:
     - High-risk Stage II: Adjuvant chemotherapy (T4, perforation, obstruction,
       fewer <12 lymph nodes, lymphovascular invasion, perineural invasion)
     - MSI-H Stage II: No benefit from 5-FU-based adjuvant chemotherapy

4. TREATMENT OF METASTATIC CRC (mCRC)

   First-Line Regimens:
   FOLFOX or CAPOX + Bevacizumab (anti-VEGF):
     - Bevacizumab 5 mg/kg IV every 2 weeks (with FOLFOX) or
       7.5 mg/kg every 3 weeks (with CAPOX)
     - Mechanism: Humanized anti-VEGF-A antibody; inhibits tumor angiogenesis
     - Used in RAS wild-type and mutant; first-line combination

   FOLFIRI + Bevacizumab:
     - Irinotecan 180 mg/m² + 5-FU/LV → bevacizumab
     - Irinotecan: Topoisomerase I inhibitor

   FOLFOX/FOLFIRI + Cetuximab or Panitumumab (RAS/RAF Wild-Type Only):
     - Cetuximab (Erbitux): Chimeric anti-EGFR IgG1 antibody;
       500 mg/m² IV every 2 weeks
     - Panitumumab (Vectibix): Fully human anti-EGFR IgG2 antibody;
       6 mg/kg IV every 2 weeks
     - ONLY for left-sided KRAS/NRAS/BRAF wild-type tumors (right-sided CRC
       has inferior response to anti-EGFR)
     - Monitoring: Skin toxicity (acneiform rash), hypomagnesemia

   FOLFOXIRI + Bevacizumab:
     - Triplet chemotherapy; for fit patients with high disease burden
     - Higher response rates; increased toxicity

   Second-Line Options:
     - FOLFIRI ± Ramucirumab (anti-VEGFR2): 8 mg/kg IV every 2 weeks
     - FOLFIRI ± Aflibercept (VEGF trap): 4 mg/kg IV every 2 weeks
     - Re-challenge regimens; TAS-102 (trifluridine/tipiracil)

5. TARGETED THERAPIES
   BRAF V600E Inhibitor Combinations:
     - Encorafenib 300 mg/day + Cetuximab: Approved for BRAF V600E mCRC
       (BEACON trial); ORR ~26%, OS improvement vs. standard therapy
     - ± Binimetinib (MEK inhibitor)

   HER2 Amplification:
     - Trastuzumab + pertuzumab; trastuzumab deruxtecan (T-DXd)
     - Tucatinib + trastuzumab (investigational/approved in some settings)

   NTRK Fusion (rare ~1%):
     - Larotrectinib (Vitrakvi) 100 mg twice daily
     - Entrectinib (Rozlytrek) 600 mg daily

6. IMMUNOTHERAPY
   Pembrolizumab (Keytruda):
     - 200 mg IV every 3 weeks or 400 mg every 6 weeks
     - First-line monotherapy for MSI-H/dMMR mCRC (KEYNOTE-177 trial)
     - Impressive durable responses; ~16-month PFS vs. ~8 months chemotherapy

   Nivolumab ± Ipilimumab:
     - For MSI-H/dMMR mCRC refractory to chemotherapy

   Dostarlimab:
     - Approved for dMMR solid tumors including CRC

7. REGORAFENIB AND TAS-102 (Late-Line Therapy)
   Regorafenib (Stivarga):
     - 160 mg/day orally (Days 1–21 of 28-day cycle)
     - Multikinase inhibitor (VEGFR, PDGFR, FGFR, KIT, RET, BRAF, TIE2)
     - 3rd/4th line mCRC; modest OS benefit

   TAS-102 / Trifluridine-Tipiracil (Lonsurf):
     - 35 mg/m² twice daily (Days 1–5 and 8–12 of 28-day cycle)
     - Late-line mCRC; oral; modest OS benefit

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SCREENING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  - Average-risk: Colonoscopy every 10 years starting at age 45 (ACS 2018
    recommendation)
  - Annual FIT test as alternative
  - High-risk (FAP): Annual sigmoidoscopy/colonoscopy from age 10–12
  - Lynch syndrome: Colonoscopy every 1–2 years from 20–25 years
  - IBD (extensive colitis ≥8 years): Surveillance colonoscopy every 1–3 years

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
MONITORING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  - CEA every 3 months × 3 years, then every 6 months × 2 years
  - CT chest/abdomen/pelvis every 6–12 months × 5 years
  - Colonoscopy at 1 year after resection, then every 3–5 years
