

Title   : Prostate Cancer — Diagnosis and Management
Source  : National Comprehensive Cancer Network (NCCN) Guidelines;
          European Association of Urology (EAU); American Cancer Society (ACS);
          American Urological Association (AUA); NIH / NCI
Disease : Prostate Cancer (Prostate Adenocarcinoma)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
OVERVIEW
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Prostate cancer is the most common non-skin malignancy and second leading cause
of cancer death in men worldwide, with approximately 1.4 million new cases and
375,000 deaths annually. It arises predominantly from the peripheral zone of
the prostate gland as prostatic adenocarcinoma (>95%). Median age at diagnosis
is 66 years. Risk factors include age, African American ethnicity (highest
incidence globally), family history, and inherited mutations (BRCA2, BRCA1,
Lynch syndrome genes). Androgen signaling drives tumor growth; the androgen
receptor (AR) axis is the primary therapeutic target. Disease ranges from
indolent, organ-confined tumors (curable with local therapy) to lethal
metastatic castration-resistant prostate cancer (mCRPC).

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SYMPTOMS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Localized Disease (often asymptomatic):
  - Detected incidentally via PSA screening
  - Lower urinary tract symptoms (LUTS): frequency, urgency, hesitancy,
    weak stream, nocturia (indistinguishable from BPH)
  - Hematuria, hematospermia (uncommon)

Locally Advanced Disease:
  - Obstructive uropathy, hydronephrosis
  - Rectal symptoms (posterior invasion)
  - Perineal pain

Metastatic Disease:
  - Bone pain (axial skeleton: spine, pelvis, ribs — osteoblastic metastases)
  - Pathological fractures
  - Spinal cord compression (oncological emergency)
  - Constitutional: fatigue, weight loss, anemia
  - Lower extremity edema (lymph node obstruction)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
DIAGNOSIS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PSA (Prostate-Specific Antigen):
  - Serine protease produced by prostatic epithelium
  - Normal: <4.0 ng/mL (age-adjusted norms)
  - PSA density, PSA velocity, free:total PSA ratio improve specificity
  - PSA is not cancer-specific (elevated in BPH, prostatitis, trauma)

Digital Rectal Exam (DRE):
  - Hard, nodular, asymmetric prostate — suspicious for cancer

Prostate Biopsy:
  - Trans-rectal ultrasound (TRUS)-guided systematic biopsy: 12-core
  - MRI-targeted (fusion biopsy): Preferred for better detection of clinically
    significant cancer; targets PI-RADS 3–5 lesions

Multiparametric MRI (mpMRI):
  - T2, DWI, DCE sequences; PI-RADS scoring 1–5
  - Performed before biopsy in suspected localized disease
  - Detects extracapsular extension, seminal vesicle invasion

Gleason Grading / Grade Groups:
  - Gleason 6 (Grade Group 1): Well-differentiated; low risk
  - Gleason 7 (3+4; Grade Group 2): Intermediate favorable
  - Gleason 7 (4+3; Grade Group 3): Intermediate unfavorable
  - Gleason 8 (Grade Group 4): High risk
  - Gleason 9–10 (Grade Group 5): Very high risk / anaplastic

Staging:
  - Bone scan: Osteoblastic metastases; PSMA PET-CT more sensitive
  - CT chest/abdomen/pelvis: Nodal and visceral metastases
  - PSMA PET (68Ga-PSMA-11 or 18F-DCFPyL): Highly sensitive for
    nodal and distant metastases; increasingly first-line staging

Risk Stratification (AUA/NCCN):
  - Very Low: T1c, PSA <10, Gleason ≤6, <3 positive cores, PSA density <0.15
  - Low: T1–T2a, PSA <10, Gleason ≤6
  - Intermediate Favorable/Unfavorable (T2b–T2c, PSA 10–20, Gleason 7)
  - High: T3a, PSA >20, or Gleason 8+
  - Very High: T3b–T4, primary Gleason 5, multiple high-risk features
  - Metastatic (N1 or M1)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TREATMENT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

1. ACTIVE SURVEILLANCE (Low-Risk Disease)
   - Serial PSA every 3–6 months
   - Annual MRI and/or repeat biopsy every 1–5 years
   - Curative treatment initiated on reclassification to higher grade/stage
   - Appropriate for very low/low risk and select intermediate favorable risk

2. RADICAL PROSTATECTOMY
   Technique:
     - Robot-assisted radical prostatectomy (RARP): Standard of care at
       major centers (da Vinci system)
     - Open retropubic radical prostatectomy
     - Nerve-sparing technique for potency preservation when oncologically safe
   Indications: Localized/locally advanced prostate cancer; life expectancy >10 yr
   Outcomes: PSA should be undetectable (<0.1 ng/mL) post-surgery
   Pelvic lymph node dissection: Extended PLND for intermediate–high risk

3. RADIATION THERAPY

   External Beam Radiation Therapy (EBRT):
     - Intensity-Modulated Radiation Therapy (IMRT): 76–80 Gy in 38–40 fractions
       or moderate hypofractionation (60 Gy/20 fractions; 70 Gy/28 fractions)
     - Ultra-hypofractionation (SBRT): 35–40 Gy in 5 fractions for low-
       intermediate risk

   Brachytherapy:
     - Low-dose rate (LDR): Permanent iodine-125 or palladium-103 seeds
       implanted transperineally; suitable for low/favorable intermediate risk
     - High-dose rate (HDR): Temporary iridium-192 implants; boost in high-risk

   Combined EBRT + ADT:
     - Short-term ADT (6 months): Intermediate risk EBRT
     - Long-term ADT (2–3 years): High/very high risk EBRT

4. ANDROGEN DEPRIVATION THERAPY (ADT)

   Medical Castration:
   GnRH Agonists (Cause initial testosterone flare — use anti-androgen for 4 weeks):
     - Leuprolide acetate (Lupron): 7.5 mg IM monthly, 22.5 mg every 3 months,
       45 mg every 6 months
     - Goserelin (Zoladex): 3.6 mg SC monthly or 10.8 mg every 3 months
     - Triptorelin: 3.75 mg IM monthly
   GnRH Antagonists (No testosterone flare; rapid castration):
     - Degarelix (Firmagon): 240 mg SC loading, then 80 mg SC monthly
     - Relugolix (Orgovyx): 360 mg orally once, then 120 mg daily
       (oral GnRH antagonist; faster recovery of testosterone)

   Anti-androgens (Androgen Receptor Blockers):
   First-generation:
     - Bicalutamide 50 mg/day (competitive AR inhibitor; used for flare
       prevention or combined blockade)
     - Flutamide 250 mg TID; nilutamide 150 mg/day (less used)
   Second-generation (superior efficacy in mCRPC):
     - Enzalutamide (Xtandi): 160 mg/day orally; binds AR with 8× higher
       affinity; also blocks nuclear translocation and DNA binding
     - Apalutamide (Erleada): 240 mg/day; approved for nmCRPC and mCSPC
     - Darolutamide (Nubeqa): 600 mg twice daily; fewer CNS side effects;
       approved for nmCRPC and mHSPC

5. CYP17 INHIBITORS (ANDROGEN SYNTHESIS INHIBITORS)
   Abiraterone Acetate (Zytiga, Yonsa):
     - 1000 mg/day orally on empty stomach (standard) OR
       500 mg fine particle formulation with low-fat meal
     - Inhibits CYP17A1 (17α-hydroxylase/17,20-lyase) → blocks androgen
       biosynthesis in adrenal, testes, and tumor tissue
     - Always co-administered with prednisone 5 mg twice daily
       (to suppress ACTH and prevent mineralocorticoid excess)
     - Approved: mCRPC, mCSPC, high-risk localized (STAMPEDE trial)
     - Monitoring: LFTs, electrolytes, blood pressure

6. CHEMOTHERAPY
   Docetaxel (Taxotere):
     - 75 mg/m² IV every 3 weeks × 10 cycles
     - Mechanism: Taxane; stabilizes microtubules; impairs cell division
     - Indicated: mCRPC (first-line) and mCSPC with high-volume metastatic disease
       (in combination with ADT — CHAARTED/STAMPEDE trials)
     - Premedication: Dexamethasone 8 mg BID for 3 days
     - Monitoring: CBC (neutropenia), neuropathy, fluid retention

   Cabazitaxel (Jevtana):
     - 20–25 mg/m² IV every 3 weeks
     - Second-generation taxane active in docetaxel-resistant mCRPC
     - G-CSF prophylaxis recommended
     - Cabazitaxel + carboplatin: For BRCA2-mutated or ATM-mutated mCRPC

7. PARP INHIBITORS (for Homologous Recombination Repair Mutations)
   Olaparib (Lynparza):
     - 300 mg orally twice daily
     - Indicated for mCRPC with BRCA1/2, ATM, or HRR mutations (post-ADT/CYP17)
   Rucaparib (Rubraca): 600 mg orally twice daily; BRCA1/2 mCRPC
   Niraparib (Zejula): 200–300 mg/day; HRR mutations
   Talazoparib (Talzenna) + enzalutamide: Combination approved for mCRPC

8. IMMUNOTHERAPY AND TARGETED THERAPY
   Sipuleucel-T (Provenge):
     - Autologous dendritic cell vaccine pulsed with PAP-GM-CSF fusion protein
     - 3 infusions every 2 weeks; improves OS by ~4 months in asymptomatic
       or minimally symptomatic mCRPC
   Pembrolizumab (Keytruda):
     - 200 mg IV every 3 weeks
     - Approved for MSI-H/dMMR or TMB-high mCRPC (tissue-agnostic approval)
   Lutetium-177 PSMA-617 (Pluvicto):
     - Radioligand therapy: 177Lu-PSMA-617 7.4 GBq IV every 6 weeks × 6 cycles
     - Targets PSMA-expressing prostate cancer cells; delivers beta radiation
     - Approved for PSMA-positive mCRPC after ADT and taxane chemotherapy

9. BONE-TARGETED THERAPY
   Radium-223 (Xofigo):
     - Alpha-emitting radioisotope; targets osteoblastic bone metastases
     - 55 kBq/kg IV every 4 weeks × 6 doses
     - Approved for symptomatic bone-only metastatic CRPC; OS benefit
   Denosumab (Xgeva):
     - 120 mg SC every 4 weeks
     - RANK-L inhibitor; prevents skeletal-related events in bone mets
   Zoledronic Acid (Zometa):
     - 4 mg IV every 3–4 weeks
     - Bisphosphonate; reduces skeletal complications in mCRPC with bone mets

10. ADT SIDE EFFECT MANAGEMENT
    - Hot flashes: Venlafaxine, medroxyprogesterone
    - Osteoporosis: Calcium + vitamin D; denosumab 60 mg SC every 6 months
    - Sexual dysfunction: PDE5 inhibitors (sildenafil, tadalafil)
    - Metabolic syndrome/cardiovascular: Exercise, statins, dietary modification
    - Fatigue, sarcopenia: Resistance exercise, dietitian referral

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
MONITORING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  - PSA every 3–6 months during active treatment
  - Testosterone level monitoring (castrate level: <50 ng/dL = <1.7 nmol/L)
  - Imaging: PSMA PET or conventional imaging for PSA rise/progression
  - Bone density (DEXA) every 1–2 years on ADT
  - Metabolic panel, lipid profile annually on ADT
  - Cardiovascular assessment: ADT increases CV risk

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PREVENTION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  - PSA screening: ACS recommends discussion starting at 50 (average risk),
    45 (African American or family history), 40 (multiple first-degree relatives)
  - 5-alpha reductase inhibitors (finasteride, dutasteride): Reduce diagnosis
    of low-grade PCa; not routine chemopreventive recommendation
  - Diet: Mediterranean diet, low red/processed meat, high fruits and vegetables
