Title: Anemia - Treatment and Hematological Management
Source: Based on Wikipedia, Mayo Clinic, WHO, ASH Guidelines
Disease: Anemia

Anemia is a condition in which the number of red blood cells or the hemoglobin concentration within them is lower than normal, resulting in reduced oxygen-carrying capacity of the blood. It is the most common hematological disorder worldwide, affecting over 2 billion people.

CLASSIFICATION BY CAUSE
Iron deficiency anemia: most common type, caused by inadequate iron intake, poor absorption, or chronic blood loss.
Vitamin B12 deficiency anemia (pernicious anemia): caused by B12 malabsorption due to lack of intrinsic factor or dietary deficiency.
Folate deficiency anemia: caused by inadequate folate intake or malabsorption.
Anemia of chronic disease: associated with chronic inflammatory, infectious, or malignant conditions.
Hemolytic anemias: destruction of red blood cells (autoimmune, hereditary spherocytosis, G6PD deficiency, sickle cell disease, thalassemia).
Aplastic anemia: bone marrow failure with reduced production of all blood cell types.
Sideroblastic anemia: ineffective erythropoiesis with iron loading of erythroid precursors.

SYMPTOMS
Fatigue, pallor, shortness of breath on exertion, dizziness, headache, palpitations, cold extremities, and reduced exercise tolerance. Severe anemia may cause angina, heart failure, and syncope.

DIAGNOSIS
Complete blood count (CBC) with differential, reticulocyte count, peripheral blood smear, serum iron, ferritin, TIBC, vitamin B12, folate levels, Coombs test, and bone marrow biopsy in complex cases.

TREATMENT APPROACHES

Iron Deficiency Anemia:
Oral iron supplementation is first-line treatment. Ferrous sulfate 200 mg three times daily (providing 65 mg elemental iron per dose) is standard. Ferrous gluconate and ferrous fumarate are alternatives. Vitamin C taken concurrently enhances iron absorption. Treatment continues for 3 to 6 months after hemoglobin normalizes to replenish iron stores.
Intravenous iron preparations including ferric carboxymaltose, iron sucrose, low-molecular-weight iron dextran, and ferumoxytol are used when oral iron is not tolerated, absorbed, or sufficient. They rapidly replenish iron stores in a few infusion sessions.
Identifying and treating the underlying cause of iron loss (peptic ulcer, colorectal cancer, menorrhagia) is essential.

Vitamin B12 Deficiency Anemia:
Intramuscular hydroxocobalamin or cyanocobalamin injections are standard treatment for pernicious anemia and other causes of B12 malabsorption. Initial loading doses are followed by maintenance injections every 3 months for life.
High-dose oral cyanocobalamin (1000-2000 mcg daily) can achieve adequate absorption even without intrinsic factor through passive diffusion and is an alternative for patients who prefer oral therapy.
Dietary B12 deficiency is treated with oral supplementation or dietary improvement.

Folate Deficiency Anemia:
Folic acid 5 mg orally daily for 4 months corrects folate deficiency anemia. Dietary improvement with folate-rich foods (leafy greens, legumes, fortified cereals) is advised.

Anemia of Chronic Disease:
Primary treatment targets the underlying condition. Erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (epoetin, darbepoetin) increase red cell production in patients with renal anemia, cancer-related anemia, or anemia related to chemotherapy. Intravenous iron improves response to ESAs.

Hemolytic Anemias:
Autoimmune hemolytic anemia: prednisolone, rituximab, splenectomy for refractory cases.
Sickle cell disease: hydroxyurea increases fetal hemoglobin and reduces sickling, vaso-occlusive crises, and transfusion requirement. Voxelotor inhibits hemoglobin S polymerization. L-glutamine reduces oxidative stress. Crizanlizumab reduces pain crises. Regular blood transfusions and iron chelation therapy for transfusion-related iron overload. Hematopoietic stem cell transplantation is curative.
Thalassemia: Regular blood transfusions and iron chelation (deferasirox, deferoxamine, deferiprone). Stem cell transplantation is curative. Gene therapy approaches are in development.

Aplastic Anemia:
Immunosuppressive therapy with antithymocyte globulin and cyclosporine for patients who are not transplant candidates. Eltrombopag, a thrombopoietin receptor agonist, improves response rates. Hematopoietic stem cell transplantation is the treatment of choice for severe aplastic anemia in eligible patients.

Blood Transfusion:
Packed red blood cell transfusion provides immediate but temporary correction of severe symptomatic anemia. Thresholds for transfusion depend on clinical context and patient symptoms.
