================================================================================
  OBSTRUCTIVE SLEEP APNEA — DIAGNOSIS AND MANAGEMENT
================================================================================

Title   : Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA) — Diagnosis and Management
Source  : American Academy of Sleep Medicine (AASM) Clinical Guidelines;
          American Thoracic Society (ATS); NIH / NHLBI; Mayo Clinic
Disease : Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
OVERVIEW
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) is the most common sleep-related breathing
disorder, characterized by repetitive episodes of partial (hypopnea) or
complete (apnea) upper airway obstruction during sleep, resulting in
intermittent hypoxia, hypercapnia, and sleep fragmentation. OSA affects
approximately 9–38% of adults globally, with prevalence rising due to obesity
epidemics. Risk factors include obesity (BMI >30), male sex, age >40, large
neck circumference (>40 cm women, >43 cm men), craniofacial abnormalities,
upper airway soft tissue enlargement (enlarged tonsils, macroglossia), alcohol
and sedative use, and family history. Untreated OSA is associated with
hypertension, cardiovascular disease, stroke, type 2 diabetes, metabolic
syndrome, motor vehicle accidents, and impaired quality of life.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SYMPTOMS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Nocturnal Symptoms:
  - Loud, habitual snoring
  - Witnessed apneas (breathing cessation observed by bed partner)
  - Gasping or choking arousals
  - Nocturia (frequent nighttime urination)
  - Diaphoresis (night sweats)
  - Restless sleep, insomnia

Daytime Symptoms:
  - Excessive daytime sleepiness (EDS) — cardinal symptom
  - Morning headaches (from hypercapnia/hypoxia)
  - Unrefreshing sleep
  - Cognitive impairment: memory loss, poor concentration
  - Mood changes: irritability, depression, anxiety
  - Decreased libido, erectile dysfunction

Children (Pediatric OSA):
  - Mouth breathing, snoring, restless sleep
  - Behavioral problems, attention-deficit hyperactivity
  - Enuresis (bedwetting)
  - Poor school performance

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
DIAGNOSIS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Screening Tools:
  - STOP-BANG Questionnaire (Snoring, Tired, Observed apneas, Pressure,
    BMI >35, Age >50, Neck >40 cm, Gender male): Score 0–2 low risk,
    3–4 intermediate, 5–8 high risk
  - Epworth Sleepiness Scale (ESS): Score >10 indicates significant sleepiness

Diagnostic Testing:
  Polysomnography (PSG) — Gold Standard:
    - In-laboratory, full-night study monitoring EEG, EOG, EMG, airflow,
      respiratory effort, SpO2, ECG, body position
    - Apnea-Hypopnea Index (AHI): apneas + hypopneas per hour of sleep
      · Mild OSA: AHI 5–14.9/hour
      · Moderate OSA: AHI 15–29.9/hour
      · Severe OSA: AHI ≥30/hour
    - Oxygen Desaturation Index (ODI): desaturations >3–4% per hour

  Home Sleep Apnea Testing (HSAT):
    - Type 3 devices: airflow, respiratory effort, SpO2 — appropriate for
      adults with high pre-test probability and no comorbidities
    - Not suitable for: suspected central sleep apnea, severe cardiopulmonary
      disease, or hypoventilation syndromes

  Additional Evaluation:
    - Lateral cephalometry or CT/MRI for anatomical assessment
    - Drug-Induced Sleep Endoscopy (DISE) for surgical planning
    - Thyroid function tests (hypothyroidism associated with OSA)
    - Fasting glucose, HbA1c, lipid panel (metabolic comorbidities)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TREATMENT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

1. POSITIVE AIRWAY PRESSURE (PAP) THERAPY — First-Line Treatment

   Continuous Positive Airway Pressure (CPAP):
     - Delivers fixed pressure (4–20 cm H2O) to pneumatically splint the
       upper airway open during sleep
     - Gold standard for moderate-to-severe OSA (AHI ≥15) and symptomatic
       mild OSA
     - Pressure titrated via PSG or auto-titrating CPAP (APAP)
     - Reduces AHI, improves daytime sleepiness, blood pressure, quality of
       life, and cardiovascular risk
     - Adherence target: ≥4 hours/night on ≥70% of nights

   Auto-titrating PAP (APAP):
     - Adjusts pressure breath-by-breath within a set range (e.g., 4–20 cm H2O)
     - Preferred for uncomplicated OSA; increases comfort and adherence

   Bilevel PAP (BiPAP / BPAP):
     - Separate inspiratory (IPAP) and expiratory (EPAP) pressures
     - Used when CPAP pressure is poorly tolerated, or in OSA with
       hypoventilation, central apneas, or neuromuscular disease

   Adaptive Servo-Ventilation (ASV):
     - For complex/central sleep apnea syndromes
     - Contraindicated in heart failure with reduced EF <45%

   CPAP Adherence Enhancement:
     - Heated humidification (reduces mouth/nasal dryness)
     - Mask fitting optimization (nasal, nasal pillow, full-face masks)
     - Ramp feature (starts at lower pressure, gradually increases)
     - Desensitization/acclimatization protocols
     - Cognitive behavioral therapy for CPAP (CBT-CPAP)
     - Telemedicine and data-driven coaching (adherence monitoring via
       SD card/wireless data download)

2. ORAL APPLIANCE THERAPY (OAT)
   - Mandibular Advancement Device (MAD): repositions mandible and tongue
     anteriorly to enlarge retropalatal and retroglossal airway
   - Indicated for: mild-to-moderate OSA, CPAP intolerance, positional OSA
   - Custom-fitted by trained dental sleep medicine specialist
   - Efficacy: AHI reduction ~50%; less effective than CPAP in severe OSA
   - Side effects: tooth soreness, temporomandibular joint discomfort,
     excessive salivation, dental movement

3. LIFESTYLE MODIFICATIONS
   Weight Loss:
     - 10% weight loss can reduce AHI by 26%
     - Bariatric surgery (BMI >35 with comorbidities): most effective
       weight-loss intervention; can achieve near-remission of OSA
   Positional Therapy:
     - Supine-predominant OSA: Positional devices, lateral sleep positioning
     - Tennis ball technique, positional alarms/vibrating devices
   Alcohol and Sedative Avoidance:
     - Alcohol relaxes pharyngeal muscles; avoid within 3–4 hours of sleep
     - Avoid benzodiazepines, opioids, and muscle relaxants before sleep
   Exercise:
     - Regular aerobic exercise reduces OSA severity independent of weight loss
   Sleep Hygiene:
     - Consistent sleep-wake schedule; adequate sleep duration

4. PHARMACOTHERAPY (Adjunctive — Not First-Line)
   Solriamfetol (Sunosi):
     - Dopamine/norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor
     - 37.5–150 mg orally once daily in morning
     - For residual EDS despite adequate CPAP adherence
   Modafinil (Provigil):
     - Wakefulness-promoting agent
     - 200–400 mg orally once daily in morning
     - For residual sleepiness; does not treat underlying OSA
   Armodafinil (Nuvigil):
     - R-enantiomer of modafinil; 150–250 mg/day
   Pitolisant (Wakix):
     - Histamine H3 receptor antagonist/inverse agonist
     - 8.9–35.6 mg/day for EDS in OSA
   Acetazolamide:
     - Carbonic anhydrase inhibitor; used in central/high-altitude sleep apnea
     - 125–250 mg twice daily
   Topical Nasal Therapies:
     - Intranasal corticosteroids (e.g., fluticasone 50 mcg/nostril/day) for
       coexistent allergic rhinitis/nasal congestion

5. SURGICAL TREATMENT
   Upper Airway Surgery:
     - Tonsillectomy and adenoidectomy: First-line in children; effective in
       adults with tonsillar hypertrophy
     - Uvulopalatopharyngoplasty (UPPP): Removes excess tissue from soft
       palate and oropharynx; 50–60% success rate
     - Maxillomandibular advancement (MMA): Most effective surgical option
       in adults; success rate 75–90%
     - Genioglossus advancement, hyoid suspension

   Hypoglossal Nerve Stimulation (HNS — Inspire Therapy):
     - Implantable device that stimulates XII cranial nerve to protrude tongue
       during inspiration
     - Indicated for moderate-to-severe OSA (AHI 15–65), CPAP intolerant,
       BMI <32, without concentric palatal collapse
     - Response rate: ~80% with AHI reduction >50%

   Nasal Surgery:
     - Septoplasty, turbinate reduction: Improves nasal airway; adjunctive
       to CPAP or OAT rather than standalone treatment

   Tracheotomy:
     - Reserved for life-threatening OSA refractory to all other treatments

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
MONITORING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  - Follow-up in 4–8 weeks after CPAP initiation with adherence/efficacy data
  - Annual review of CPAP pressure, mask fit, and adherence
  - Repeat PSG if weight change >10%, or clinical deterioration
  - Blood pressure monitoring (OSA is a secondary cause of hypertension)
  - Cardiovascular and metabolic risk assessment annually
  - Driving safety counseling (significant crash risk when untreated)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
COMPLICATIONS OF UNTREATED OSA
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  - Systemic hypertension (in 50% of OSA patients)
  - Pulmonary hypertension
  - Atrial fibrillation and cardiac arrhythmias
  - Heart failure, coronary artery disease
  - Stroke (2–4× increased risk)
  - Type 2 diabetes and insulin resistance
  - Depression and anxiety
  - Increased motor vehicle accident risk (7× higher)
  - Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD)
