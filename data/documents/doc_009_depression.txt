Title: Major Depressive Disorder - Treatment, Therapy, and Management
Source: Based on Wikipedia, Mayo Clinic, NIMH, APA Guidelines
Disease: Major Depressive Disorder (MDD)

Major depressive disorder (MDD) is a common and serious mental health condition characterized by persistent depressed mood, loss of interest in activities, and a range of emotional and physical symptoms that significantly impair daily functioning. It is a leading cause of disability worldwide.

OVERVIEW
Depression involves dysregulation of neurotransmitter systems (serotonin, norepinephrine, dopamine), disrupted HPA axis function, neuroinflammation, and structural brain changes. The exact etiology is multifactorial, involving genetic, biological, psychological, and social factors.

DIAGNOSIS
Diagnosis requires at least five symptoms from the DSM-5 criteria present for at least two weeks, including depressed mood or loss of interest, along with changes in sleep, appetite, energy, concentration, psychomotor activity, and thoughts of death or suicide.

TREATMENT APPROACHES

Psychotherapy:
Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) is the most extensively studied and effective psychotherapy for depression. It helps patients identify and modify negative thought patterns and behaviors.
Interpersonal Therapy (IPT) focuses on improving interpersonal relationships and communication skills.
Behavioral Activation Therapy encourages engagement in rewarding activities to break the cycle of withdrawal and low mood.
Psychodynamic therapy explores unconscious conflicts and early experiences contributing to depression.

Antidepressant Medications:
Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors (SSRIs): First-line medications including fluoxetine, sertraline, escitalopram, citalopram, paroxetine, and fluvoxamine. They block serotonin reuptake, increasing synaptic serotonin levels. They are generally well-tolerated with a favorable side effect profile.
Serotonin-Norepinephrine Reuptake Inhibitors (SNRIs): Venlafaxine, duloxetine, and desvenlafaxine block both serotonin and norepinephrine reuptake. Duloxetine is also used for pain conditions comorbid with depression.
Atypical Antidepressants: Bupropion inhibits norepinephrine and dopamine reuptake, is activating, and has no sexual side effects. Mirtazapine is sedating and useful for patients with insomnia and poor appetite.
Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCAs): Amitriptyline, nortriptyline, and imipramine are older agents effective for depression but with more side effects and lethality in overdose.
Monoamine Oxidase Inhibitors (MAOIs): Phenelzine and tranylcypromine are reserved for treatment-resistant cases due to dietary restrictions and drug interactions.

Augmentation Strategies:
When monotherapy is inadequate, adding lithium, atypical antipsychotics (aripiprazole, quetiapine, brexpiprazole), thyroid hormone, or buspirone to an antidepressant can improve response.

Newer Treatments:
Esketamine (Spravato) nasal spray is an NMDA receptor antagonist approved for treatment-resistant depression, providing rapid antidepressant effects.
Ketamine infusion therapy is used off-label for treatment-resistant and suicidal depression.
Brexanolone is approved for postpartum depression.

Neuromodulation:
Electroconvulsive therapy (ECT) remains the most effective treatment for severe, treatment-resistant, or psychotic depression and has rapid onset of action.
Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) uses magnetic pulses to stimulate neural circuits involved in mood regulation, approved for treatment-resistant depression.

Lifestyle Interventions:
Regular aerobic exercise has antidepressant effects comparable to medication for mild to moderate depression. Adequate sleep hygiene, social support, stress management, and nutrition also play important roles.

DURATION OF TREATMENT
Most guidelines recommend continuing antidepressants for at least 6 to 12 months after remission to prevent relapse. Patients with recurrent depression may require long-term maintenance treatment.
