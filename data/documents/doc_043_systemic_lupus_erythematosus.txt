================================================================================
  SYSTEMIC LUPUS ERYTHEMATOSUS — DIAGNOSIS AND MANAGEMENT
================================================================================

Title   : Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE) — Diagnosis and Management
Source  : European League Against Rheumatism (EULAR) Recommendations;
          American College of Rheumatology (ACR); Lupus Foundation of America;
          NIH / NIAMS; UpToDate Clinical Reference
Disease : Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
OVERVIEW
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) is a chronic, multisystem autoimmune
disease characterized by dysregulated immune responses, autoantibody production
(particularly anti-dsDNA and anti-Smith), complement consumption, and immune
complex deposition in target organs. SLE predominantly affects women of
childbearing age (female:male ratio 9:1), with peak incidence between ages
15–45. Global prevalence is 20–150 per 100,000, higher in African, Asian, and
Hispanic populations. Pathogenesis involves a combination of genetic
predisposition (HLA-DR2, HLA-DR3, complement deficiencies), environmental
triggers (UV light, infections, hormones, drugs), and epigenetic dysregulation.
The resulting loss of self-tolerance leads to B-cell hyperactivity, autoreactive
T cells, and multiorgan inflammation affecting the kidneys, skin, joints,
serosal surfaces, CNS, and blood.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SYMPTOMS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Constitutional:
  - Fatigue (most common symptom, >90%)
  - Fever, weight loss, lymphadenopathy

Mucocutaneous (70–80%):
  - Malar (butterfly) rash: erythematous rash across cheeks/nose, spares
    nasolabial folds; photosensitive
  - Discoid lupus: scarring plaques on sun-exposed areas
  - Subacute cutaneous lupus: annular/papulosquamous photosensitive rash
  - Oral/nasal ulcers (usually painless)
  - Hair loss (diffuse alopecia)
  - Raynaud's phenomenon

Musculoskeletal (>90%):
  - Arthralgia and non-erosive arthritis (symmetric, polyarticular)
  - Jaccoud's arthropathy (reducible deformities)
  - Myalgia, myositis (less common)

Renal — Lupus Nephritis (50%):
  - Proteinuria, hematuria, cellular casts
  - Hypertension, peripheral edema
  - Reduced GFR progressing to renal failure

Neuropsychiatric (20–40%):
  - Headache, seizures, psychosis
  - Cognitive dysfunction, mood disorders
  - Cerebrovascular disease, myelopathy
  - Peripheral and cranial neuropathies

Cardiopulmonary:
  - Pleuritis (pleuritic chest pain, effusion)
  - Pericarditis, myocarditis
  - Libman-Sacks endocarditis (sterile verrucous lesions)
  - Accelerated atherosclerosis

Hematological:
  - Hemolytic anemia, leukopenia, lymphopenia, thrombocytopenia

Antiphospholipid Syndrome (secondary):
  - Thrombosis (arterial/venous), recurrent pregnancy loss

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
DIAGNOSIS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Classification Criteria (2019 EULAR/ACR Criteria):
  - Entry criterion: ANA titer ≥1:80 (required)
  - Seven domains with weighted criteria; score ≥10 classifies as SLE
  - Domains: constitutional, hematological, neuropsychiatric, mucocutaneous,
    serosal, musculoskeletal, renal, immunological

Key Autoantibodies:
  - Antinuclear antibody (ANA): Sensitive (~99%) but not specific; screening test
  - Anti-dsDNA: Specific (>97%), correlates with disease activity and nephritis
  - Anti-Smith (anti-Sm): Highly specific (>99%), does not correlate with activity
  - Anti-Ro/SSA, anti-La/SSB: Associated with neonatal lupus, subacute cutaneous
    lupus, secondary Sjögren's
  - Anti-phospholipid antibodies (aCL, β2-GP1, lupus anticoagulant):
    Antiphospholipid syndrome
  - Anti-histone: Drug-induced lupus

Complement Studies:
  - Low C3, C4: Active disease (consumed by immune complexes)

Lupus Nephritis Assessment:
  - Urine protein:creatinine ratio (UPCR), 24-hour urine protein
  - Urinalysis with microscopy (red cell casts = Class III–IV nephritis)
  - Renal biopsy: Gold standard; ISN/RPS classification:
    Class I (minimal mesangial) → Class VI (advanced sclerosing)

Other Tests:
  - CBC, CMP, ESR, CRP (usually normal in flare)
  - Coombs test (hemolytic anemia)
  - SLEDAI or BILAG scores for disease activity assessment

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TREATMENT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

1. ANTIMALARIALS — Backbone of SLE Therapy
   Hydroxychloroquine (HCQ, Plaquenil):
     - Mechanism: Toll-like receptor inhibition; interferes with antigen
       presentation and immune activation
     - Dose: 200–400 mg/day orally (weight-based: ≤5 mg/kg/day)
     - Indicated in ALL SLE patients without contraindication
     - Reduces flare frequency, cumulative organ damage, and mortality
     - Cardioprotective and antithrombotic effects
     - Monitoring: Annual ophthalmology exam (retinal toxicity after 5+ years)
   Chloroquine:
     - 250 mg/day; alternative when HCQ unavailable

2. NON-STEROIDAL ANTI-INFLAMMATORY DRUGS (NSAIDs)
   - For arthralgia, serositis, mild musculoskeletal symptoms
   - Naproxen 250–500 mg twice daily; ibuprofen 400–800 mg TID
   - Use cautiously in lupus nephritis (renal prostaglandin effects)

3. CORTICOSTEROIDS
   Mechanism: Broad immunosuppression via glucocorticoid receptor
   Indications and Doses:
     - Mild-moderate disease: Prednisone/prednisolone 5–15 mg/day orally
     - Moderate-severe flare: 0.5–1 mg/kg/day oral prednisone
     - Severe/life-threatening (nephritis, CNS, cytopenias):
       Methylprednisolone IV pulse 500–1000 mg/day × 3 days
     - Topical for cutaneous disease: Triamcinolone, betamethasone
   Tapering: Taper to lowest effective dose; goal <7.5 mg/day prednisolone
   Glucocorticoid-sparing agents initiated alongside for long-term use

4. CONVENTIONAL IMMUNOSUPPRESSANTS
   Hydroxychloroquine (see above)

   Azathioprine (Imuran):
     - Mechanism: Purine analog; inhibits T and B cell proliferation
     - Dose: 1–2.5 mg/kg/day orally
     - Indications: Mild-moderate disease, lupus nephritis maintenance,
       pregnancy-compatible immunosuppression
     - Check TPMT enzyme activity before initiation
     - Monitoring: CBC (myelosuppression), LFTs

   Mycophenolate Mofetil (MMF, CellCept):
     - Mechanism: Inhibits inosine monophosphate dehydrogenase (IMPDH);
       selective B and T cell antiproliferative
     - Dose: 2–3 g/day orally in divided doses (induction); 1–2 g/day
       (maintenance)
     - First-line for lupus nephritis Class III–V induction and maintenance
     - Monitoring: CBC, LFTs, renal function; teratogenic — contraception required

   Cyclophosphamide (Cytoxan):
     - Mechanism: Alkylating agent; cross-links DNA in rapidly dividing
       immune cells
     - IV pulse regimen (NIH): 0.5–1 g/m² monthly × 6 doses (induction for
       severe nephritis, CNS lupus)
     - Euro-Lupus low-dose regimen: 500 mg IV every 2 weeks × 6 doses
     - Monitoring: Urinalysis (hemorrhagic cystitis — use mesna prophylaxis),
       CBC, gonadal toxicity; strongly teratogenic

   Methotrexate (MTX):
     - 7.5–20 mg/week orally/SC; folic acid 1 mg/day supplementation
     - For articular and cutaneous disease
     - Contraindicated in pregnancy and renal impairment

   Calcineurin Inhibitors:
     - Tacrolimus 2–4 mg/day or cyclosporine 2.5–5 mg/kg/day
     - Used in refractory lupus nephritis, especially membranous (Class V)

5. BIOLOGIC THERAPY
   Belimumab (Benlysta):
     - Mechanism: Human monoclonal antibody against BAFF (B-lymphocyte
       stimulator); reduces autoreactive B cell survival
     - Dose: 10 mg/kg IV every 4 weeks (after 3 loading doses) OR
       200 mg SC weekly
     - Indicated for active SLE (excluding severe active nephritis/CNS)
     - Reduces flares and allows corticosteroid tapering

   Voclosporin (Lupkynis):
     - Calcineurin inhibitor combined with MMF for lupus nephritis
     - Dose: 23.7 mg orally twice daily
     - FDA-approved specifically for active lupus nephritis

   Anifrolumab (Saphnelo):
     - Mechanism: Anti-type I interferon receptor antibody
     - 300 mg IV every 4 weeks
     - FDA-approved for moderate-to-severe SLE; not for severe active nephritis
     - Reduces disease activity, skin and joint manifestations

   Rituximab (off-label):
     - Anti-CD20 monoclonal antibody; depletes B cells
     - 1000 mg IV × 2 doses (2 weeks apart), repeated every 6 months
     - Used for refractory SLE, severe cytopenias, refractory nephritis

6. ANTIPHOSPHOLIPID SYNDROME MANAGEMENT
   Thromboprophylaxis:
     - Low-dose aspirin 81 mg/day for aPL-positive patients without thrombosis
     - Warfarin (target INR 2–3 for venous thrombosis; 2.5–3.5 for arterial)
       as long-term anticoagulation after thrombotic event
     - Hydroxychloroquine: Antithrombotic benefit

7. SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS
   Pregnancy in SLE:
     - High-risk pregnancy; requires specialist co-management
     - Continue hydroxychloroquine (reduces neonatal lupus risk)
     - Compatible: Azathioprine, prednisolone, low-dose aspirin
     - Contraindicated: MMF, cyclophosphamide, methotrexate, belimumab
     - Monitor for pre-eclampsia, fetal growth restriction

   Osteoporosis Prevention:
     - Calcium + vitamin D supplementation for all on corticosteroids
     - Bisphosphonates for high-risk patients

   Infection Prevention:
     - Vaccinations: Inactivated influenza, pneumococcal, Tdap, hepatitis B
       (avoid live vaccines during immunosuppression)
     - PCP prophylaxis (trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole 400/80 mg daily)
       during high-dose immunosuppression

   Cardiovascular Risk Reduction:
     - Statins, antihypertensives, smoking cessation
     - ACE inhibitors/ARBs for proteinuria/nephritis

   Sun Protection:
     - SPF 30–50 broad-spectrum sunscreen; UV-protective clothing

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
MONITORING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  - Disease activity: SLEDAI, BILAG, physician global assessment
  - CBC, CMP, urinalysis, UPCR: every 1–3 months (active) or 3–6 months (stable)
  - Complement (C3, C4), anti-dsDNA: follow disease activity
  - Ophthalmology for HCQ retinopathy: annually after 5 years
  - Blood pressure, lipid profile, glucose: cardiovascular monitoring
  - Bone mineral density (DEXA): baseline and every 1–2 years on steroids

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
COMPLICATIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  - End-stage renal disease (lupus nephritis progression)
  - Cardiovascular events (MI, stroke — leading cause of late mortality)
  - Avascular necrosis of bone (corticosteroid-related)
  - Opportunistic infections (immunosuppression-related)
  - Lymphoma (slightly elevated risk)
  - Drug toxicity (HCQ retinopathy, cyclophosphamide-induced malignancy)
