Title: Gout - Treatment and Rheumatological Management
Source: Based on Wikipedia, Mayo Clinic, ACR/EULAR Guidelines
Disease: Gout

Gout is the most common form of inflammatory arthritis, caused by deposition of monosodium urate (MSU) crystals in joints and soft tissues due to sustained hyperuricemia (serum urate > 6.8 mg/dL). It is characterized by recurrent attacks of acute gouty arthritis and, if untreated, chronic tophaceous gout with joint destruction.

OVERVIEW
Uric acid is the final product of purine catabolism in humans. Hyperuricemia results from overproduction of uric acid, underexcretion by the kidneys, or both. Crystal deposition triggers intense neutrophil-mediated inflammation via NLRP3 inflammasome activation and IL-1beta release.

SYMPTOMS
Acute gout: sudden onset of severe, excruciating pain, swelling, redness, and warmth typically in a single joint. The first metatarsophalangeal joint (podagra) is classically affected, but ankles, knees, wrists, and elbows are also common sites. Attacks peak within 12-24 hours and resolve over 3-14 days.
Intercritical gout: asymptomatic periods between attacks with ongoing subclinical crystal deposition.
Chronic tophaceous gout: persistent urate crystal deposits forming tophi in joints, bursae, tendons, and subcutaneous tissues, causing joint destruction and disability.

DIAGNOSIS
Joint aspiration with polarized light microscopy demonstrating negatively birefringent needle-shaped MSU crystals is the gold standard. Ultrasound reveals characteristic double contour sign. Dual-energy CT can detect urate crystal deposits. Serum urate is supportive but may be normal during acute attacks.

TREATMENT APPROACHES

Acute Gout Attack:
NSAIDs: indomethacin, naproxen, or ibuprofen at full anti-inflammatory doses for 5-7 days are first-line if no contraindications. Should be initiated within 24 hours of attack onset for maximum efficacy.
Colchicine: Low-dose colchicine (1.2 mg followed by 0.6 mg one hour later, then 0.6 mg twice daily) is highly effective and equally efficacious to high-dose regimens with fewer gastrointestinal side effects. Best results when started within 12-24 hours of attack onset.
Corticosteroids: Prednisolone 30-35 mg/day for 5 days, or intra-articular methylprednisolone injection for monoarthritis, are used when NSAIDs and colchicine are contraindicated (renal impairment, drug interactions).
IL-1 inhibitors: Anakinra, canakinumab, or rilonacept are used for refractory acute gout when other agents are contraindicated.

Urate-Lowering Therapy (ULT):
ULT is the cornerstone of long-term gout management and reduces serum urate below the saturation threshold (target < 6 mg/dL, or < 5 mg/dL in tophaceous gout), causing crystal dissolution and preventing future attacks.

Xanthine Oxidase Inhibitors (first-line ULT):
Allopurinol is the preferred first-line ULT. It inhibits xanthine oxidase, reducing uric acid production. Starting dose of 100 mg daily is gradually uptitrated by 100 mg every 2-4 weeks to reach target serum urate. Maximum dose is 800-900 mg/day. HLA-B*5801 testing is recommended in Southeast Asian and Black patients before starting allopurinol to identify those at high risk of severe cutaneous adverse reactions (DRESS, Stevens-Johnson syndrome).
Febuxostat is a selective xanthine oxidase inhibitor used when allopurinol is not tolerated. FDA black box warning for cardiovascular events requires monitoring.

Uricosuric Agents:
Probenecid and lesinurad increase renal uric acid excretion. Lesinurad is used in combination with allopurinol for patients not at target on xanthine oxidase inhibitor alone.

Pegloticase:
Recombinant polyethylene glycol-conjugated uricase converts urate to allantoin, dramatically reducing serum urate. Used for treatment-refractory chronic tophaceous gout. Given IV every 2 weeks. Immunogenicity is a major concern. Co-administration with methotrexate reduces antibody formation and improves response durability.

LIFESTYLE MODIFICATIONS
Dietary purine reduction (limiting red meat, organ meats, shellfish, sardines), alcohol restriction (especially beer), sugar-sweetened beverage avoidance, adequate hydration, and weight loss all reduce serum urate and attack frequency. Dairy consumption and vitamin C supplementation have uricosuric effects.

Prophylaxis During ULT Initiation:
Low-dose colchicine (0.6 mg once or twice daily) or low-dose NSAIDs should be given for 3-6 months when starting ULT to prevent mobilization flares as crystals dissolve.
