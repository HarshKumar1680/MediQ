================================================================================
  ACUTE KIDNEY INJURY — DIAGNOSIS AND MANAGEMENT
================================================================================

Title   : Acute Kidney Injury (AKI) — Diagnosis and Management
Source  : KDIGO (Kidney Disease: Improving Global Outcomes) AKI Guidelines;
          American Society of Nephrology (ASN); NIH / NIDDK;
          UpToDate Clinical Reference; Critical Care Nephrology
Disease : Acute Kidney Injury (AKI) / Acute Renal Failure

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
OVERVIEW
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Acute kidney injury (AKI) is an abrupt decline in kidney function (within
hours to days) resulting in reduced GFR, accumulation of nitrogenous waste
products, and dysregulation of fluid, electrolyte, and acid-base homeostasis.
Hospital prevalence is approximately 10–15% of admitted patients; 50–60% in
ICU settings. In-hospital mortality for severe AKI ranges from 40–60% in
critically ill patients. Etiology is classified as prerenal (60–70%): reduced
renal perfusion (hypovolemia, heart failure, sepsis); intrinsic (25–40%):
acute tubular necrosis (ATN), glomerulonephritis, interstitial nephritis,
vasculitis; and postrenal (5–10%): urinary tract obstruction. ATN from
ischemia or nephrotoxins is the most common intrinsic cause. AKI is a risk
factor for subsequent CKD and ESKD development.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SYMPTOMS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Oliguria/Anuria:
  - Urine output <0.5 mL/kg/hour for >6 hours (oliguria)
  - Urine output <100 mL/day (anuria)

Fluid Overload:
  - Peripheral edema, pulmonary edema
  - Hypertension
  - Dyspnea, orthopnea

Uremia (azotemic symptoms):
  - Nausea, vomiting, anorexia
  - Altered mental status, confusion
  - Uremic frost (rare, severe)
  - Pericarditis (uremic)
  - Coagulopathy

Electrolyte Disturbances:
  - Hyperkalemia: Weakness, paresthesias, arrhythmia
  - Hyponatremia: Headache, confusion, seizures
  - Hyperphosphatemia, hypocalcemia: Muscle cramps, tetany
  - Metabolic acidosis: Kussmaul breathing, confusion

Cause-Specific Features:
  - Prerenal: Thirst, orthostatic hypotension, reduced skin turgor
  - Obstruction: Flank pain, distended bladder, hematuria
  - Glomerulonephritis: Hematuria, proteinuria, hypertension

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
DIAGNOSIS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
KDIGO Diagnostic Criteria (any one of):
  - Serum creatinine rise ≥0.3 mg/dL within 48 hours
  - Serum creatinine ≥1.5× baseline within 7 days
  - Urine output <0.5 mL/kg/hour for ≥6 hours

KDIGO Staging:
  Stage 1: SCr ×1.5–1.9 baseline or +0.3 mg/dL; UO <0.5 mL/kg/hr × 6–12 hr
  Stage 2: SCr ×2.0–2.9 baseline; UO <0.5 mL/kg/hr × ≥12 hr
  Stage 3: SCr ×3.0 baseline or ≥4.0 mg/dL or RRT initiated; UO <0.3 ml/kg/hr
            × ≥24 hr or anuria ≥12 hr

Laboratory:
  - BMP (BUN, creatinine, electrolytes, bicarbonate, calcium, phosphate)
  - CBC, coagulation (DIC)
  - Urinalysis with microscopy:
    · Prerenal: Normal sediment; hyaline casts; high SG (>1.020)
    · ATN: Muddy brown granular casts; renal tubular epithelial cells;
      isosthenuric (SG ~1.010)
    · Glomerulonephritis: RBC casts, dysmorphic red cells, proteinuria
    · Interstitial nephritis: WBC casts, eosinophiluria, mild proteinuria
  - Urine electrolytes: FENa = (UNa × SCr)/(SNa × UCr) × 100
    · FENa <1%: Prerenal; >2%: Intrinsic (ATN)
    · FEUrea <35%: Prerenal (useful in patients on diuretics)
  - Urine sodium (<20 mEq/L: prerenal; >40: intrinsic)

Imaging:
  - Renal ultrasound: Size, echogenicity, obstruction (hydronephrosis),
    resistive index (RI >0.7 = poor renal vascular flow)
  - CT without contrast: Stones, masses, obstruction

Additional Tests (if intrinsic AKI suspected):
  - ANA, anti-dsDNA, ANCA (pANCA, cANCA), anti-GBM antibodies
  - Complement (C3, C4)
  - Serum protein electrophoresis (myeloma)
  - Blood cultures (sepsis-associated AKI)
  - HIV, hepatitis B/C (glomerulonephritis)

Kidney Biopsy:
  - For unexplained intrinsic AKI without clear diagnosis
  - Required for suspected rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis (RPGN)
  - Contraindicated if coagulopathy, single kidney, uncontrolled HTN

Novel Biomarkers:
  - Urine NGAL (neutrophil gelatinase-associated lipocalin): Early ATN marker
  - Urine KIM-1 (kidney injury molecule-1)
  - Urine IL-18, liver-type FABP
  - Insulin-like growth factor-binding protein 7 (IGFBP7) × tissue inhibitor
    of metalloproteinase-2 (TIMP-2) — FDA-cleared (NephroCheck) for AKI risk

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TREATMENT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

1. IDENTIFY AND TREAT UNDERLYING CAUSE

   Prerenal AKI — Volume Resuscitation:
     - Isotonic crystalloids: Normal saline (0.9% NaCl) or balanced solutions
       (Ringer's lactate, Plasmalyte) 500 mL bolus IV, reassess
     - Balanced crystalloids preferred (lower risk of hyperchloremic acidosis):
       Plasmalyte or Ringer's lactate
     - Albumin 4% or 20%: For cirrhosis-associated AKI (HRS-AKI), hypoalbuminemia
     - Vasopressors (septic/vasodilatory shock): Norepinephrine first-line;
       maintain MAP ≥65 mmHg
     - Treat underlying heart failure, hepatorenal syndrome

   Intrinsic AKI — ATN:
     - Remove/reduce nephrotoxins (see below)
     - Optimize hemodynamics and renal perfusion
     - Supportive management; no specific reversal therapy for ATN

   Glomerulonephritis (RPGN):
     - Immunosuppression urgently: Pulse methylprednisolone 500–1000 mg IV
       × 3 days, then oral prednisolone 1 mg/kg/day
     - Cyclophosphamide 2 mg/kg/day (ANCA vasculitis, anti-GBM)
     - Rituximab (anti-CD20): ANCA vasculitis — 375 mg/m² weekly × 4 doses
       (non-inferior to cyclophosphamide)
     - Plasma exchange: Anti-GBM (Goodpasture's) disease and severe ANCA

   Acute Interstitial Nephritis (AIN):
     - Withdraw offending drug (most common: NSAIDs, PPIs, antibiotics,
       antibiotics, allopurinol)
     - Prednisolone 0.5–1 mg/kg/day if no improvement within 1–2 weeks

   Obstructive AKI (Postrenal):
     - Bladder catheter for bladder outlet obstruction
     - Nephrostomy tube or ureteral stent for ureteral obstruction
     - Treat underlying cause (stones, malignancy, BPH)

2. FLUID MANAGEMENT
   - Restrictive fluid strategy in anuric/oliguric AKI to avoid overload
   - Fluid challenges guided by dynamic markers (pulse pressure variation,
     stroke volume variation, passive leg raise test)
   - Loop diuretics (Furosemide 40–200 mg IV): For fluid overload management;
     does NOT reduce AKI duration but facilitates fluid balance
   - Furosemide stress test (FST): 1–1.5 mg/kg IV; predict renal recovery
     (adequate response >200 mL in 2 hours)

3. ELECTROLYTE MANAGEMENT

   Hyperkalemia Treatment (K+ >6.0 mEq/L or ECG changes):
     Priority: Stabilize cardiac membrane → Shift K+ intracellularly → Eliminate
     a) Calcium gluconate 10% 10–20 mL IV over 10 min: Cardiac membrane
        stabilization (does NOT lower K+); ECG monitoring
     b) Regular insulin 10 units IV + Dextrose 50% 50 mL (if normoglycemic):
        Shifts K+ into cells; lasts 4–6 hours; lowers K+ by 0.5–1.5 mEq/L
     c) Sodium bicarbonate 50–100 mEq IV: In metabolic acidosis; shifts K+
     d) Albuterol (Salbutamol) 10–20 mg nebulized: Shifts K+ intracellularly
     e) Patiromer (Veltassa) 8.4 g orally: Non-absorbed K+ binder (stool);
        onset 7 hours; for chronic hyperkalemia
     f) Sodium zirconium cyclosilicate (Lokelma) 10 g TID × 48 hours, then
        maintenance: Fast-acting K+ binder (1 hour onset)
     g) Sodium polystyrene sulfonate (Kayexalate): Historically used;
        efficacy questioned; risk of intestinal necrosis
     h) Renal replacement therapy: Severe/refractory hyperkalemia

   Metabolic Acidosis:
     - Sodium bicarbonate: IV in severe acidosis (pH <7.1 or HCO3 <10 mEq/L)
     - 1 mEq/kg IV bolus; infusion 150 mEq in 1L D5W
     - Caution: Hypernatremia, volume overload, paradoxical intracellular acidosis

   Hyponatremia:
     - Fluid restriction in dilutional hyponatremia
     - Hypertonic saline (3% NaCl) for severe symptomatic hyponatremia
     - Correct no faster than 8–10 mEq/L per 24 hours (osmotic demyelination risk)

4. NEPHROTOXIN AVOIDANCE AND PREVENTION
   - Avoid/minimize nephrotoxic drugs: NSAIDs, aminoglycosides, contrast agents,
     vancomycin, calcineurin inhibitors, cidofovir, tenofovir
   - IV contrast-induced AKI prevention:
     · Adequate hydration pre/post (normal saline 1 mL/kg/hour × 6–12 hr)
     · Use iso-osmolar or low-osmolar contrast
     · Hold metformin 48 hours pre-contrast
     · N-acetylcysteine (NAC): 600–1200 mg BID × 2 days — evidence mixed
   - Aminoglycoside dosing: Once-daily dosing and AUC-based monitoring
   - Vancomycin: AUC/MIC dosing (target 400–600 mg·h/L); avoid trough
     monitoring alone

5. NUTRITIONAL SUPPORT
   - Caloric intake: 20–25 kcal/kg/day (non-catabolic) or 25–30 (hypercatabolic)
   - Protein: 0.8–1.0 g/kg/day (non-dialysis); 1.2–1.7 g/kg/day (RRT)
   - Enteral nutrition preferred over parenteral
   - Avoid hyperglycemia (target glucose 110–150 mg/dL)
   - Restrict potassium, phosphate, sodium as needed

6. RENAL REPLACEMENT THERAPY (RRT)

   Indications (AEIOU):
     A — Acidosis (pH <7.1 refractory to bicarbonate)
     E — Electrolytes (severe refractory hyperkalemia >6.5 mEq/L)
     I — Intoxication (dialyzable: lithium, methanol, ethylene glycol, salicylates)
     O — Overload (fluid overload refractory to diuretics)
     U — Uremia (symptomatic: encephalopathy, pericarditis, bleeding)

   RRT Modalities:
   Intermittent Hemodialysis (IHD):
     - 3–5 sessions/week, 3–4 hours each
     - Blood flow 250–400 mL/min; dialysate flow 500–800 mL/min
     - More efficient solute clearance; preferred for stable patients
     - Anticoagulation: Unfractionated heparin (UFH) or citrate

   Continuous Renal Replacement Therapy (CRRT):
     - Continuous Veno-Venous Hemofiltration (CVVH): Convection
     - Continuous Veno-Venous Hemodialysis (CVVHD): Diffusion
     - Continuous Veno-Venous Hemodiafiltration (CVVHDF): Combined
     - Dose: 20–25 mL/kg/hour effluent; ≥35 mL/kg/hr does NOT improve outcomes
     - Anticoagulation: Regional citrate (preferred); prostacyclin; UFH
     - Preferred for hemodynamically unstable ICU patients

   Sustained Low-Efficiency Dialysis (SLED):
     - 6–12 hour sessions at lower blood/dialysate flow rates
     - Intermediate modality; suitable for hemodynamically unstable patients

   Peritoneal Dialysis (PD):
     - Used in AKI when vascular access impossible or in low-resource settings
     - Manual or automated exchanges via peritoneal catheter

   Timing of RRT:
     - Based on clinical indications (AEIOU criteria) rather than creatinine
       threshold alone (STARRT-AKI trial: no benefit to early initiation
       without clear indication)

7. HEPATORENAL SYNDROME (HRS-AKI)
   - AKI in cirrhosis with ascites; functional vasoconstriction (not structural)
   - Treat precipitants: Infections, GI bleeding, excessive diuresis
   - Terlipressin 0.5–2 mg IV every 4–6 hours + Albumin 20–40 g/day:
     First-line vasoconstrictive therapy (Type 1 HRS)
   - Norepinephrine (0.5–3 mg/hour) + albumin: ICU setting for HRS
   - Midodrine (5–15 mg TID) + octreotide (100–200 mcg TID SC) + albumin:
     Alternative/outpatient
   - Liver transplantation: Definitive treatment

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
MONITORING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  - Serum creatinine, electrolytes, BUN: Daily (or more frequently in ICU)
  - Urine output: Hourly in critical AKI
  - Fluid balance: Daily weight
  - 12-lead ECG: If hyperkalemia
  - Renal recovery: Creatinine trending; RRT weaning trial when UO >500 mL/day

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
PREVENTION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  - Optimize fluid status perioperatively
  - Avoid nephrotoxic combinations (NSAIDs + ACE inhibitor + diuretic = triple
    whammy — high AKI risk)
  - Contrast nephropathy prophylaxis
  - Prompt treatment of sepsis (Surviving Sepsis Campaign)
  - Bundle care: KDIGO AKI Care Bundle (Stage 2–3)
  - Post-AKI follow-up: 3-month nephrology review; CKD surveillance
