================================================================================
  BIPOLAR DISORDER — DIAGNOSIS AND MANAGEMENT
================================================================================

Title   : Bipolar Disorder — Diagnosis and Management
Source  : American Psychiatric Association (APA) Practice Guidelines;
          CANMAT/ISBD Bipolar Disorder Guidelines; BAP Guidelines;
          NIH / NIMH; DSM-5-TR
Disease : Bipolar Disorder (Manic-Depressive Illness)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
OVERVIEW
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Bipolar disorder (BD) is a chronic, episodic mood disorder characterized by
recurrent episodes of mania/hypomania and depression, with periods of normal
mood (euthymia) between episodes. Lifetime prevalence is approximately 1–4%
(BD-I ~1%; BD-II ~1.1%; Cyclothymia ~2.4%). It ranks among the leading causes
of disability worldwide, with peak onset in late adolescence to early adulthood.
BD-I is defined by at least one manic episode (with or without depression);
BD-II by hypomanic and major depressive episodes without full mania. Etiology
involves genetic predisposition (heritability 60–85%; CACNA1C, ANK3, CLOCK
gene associations), neurobiological dysregulation of monoamine systems, HPA
axis abnormalities, circadian rhythm disruption, and environmental precipitants
(sleep deprivation, substance use, stressful life events).

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SYMPTOMS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Manic Episode (BD-I; ≥7 days or any duration if hospitalized):
  - Elevated, expansive, or irritable mood
  - Increased energy, goal-directed activity, psychomotor agitation
  - Decreased need for sleep (feels rested after 3 hours)
  - Grandiosity or inflated self-esteem
  - Racing thoughts, flight of ideas, pressure of speech
  - Distractibility
  - Risky behavior: sexual indiscretion, spending sprees, impulsive decisions
  - Psychotic features in severe mania (delusions of grandeur, hallucinations)

Hypomanic Episode (BD-II; ≥4 days):
  - Similar to mania but less severe; does not cause marked impairment
  - No psychosis; no hospitalization required

Major Depressive Episode (≥2 weeks):
  - Depressed mood or anhedonia
  - Neurovegetative symptoms: sleep (usually hypersomnia), appetite changes,
    psychomotor retardation or agitation
  - Fatigue, poor concentration
  - Feelings of worthlessness, guilt
  - Suicidal ideation (high-risk: lifetime suicidality ~25–50% in BD)

Mixed Features:
  - Simultaneous depressive and (hypo)manic symptoms
  - Associated with higher suicidality and substance use

Rapid Cycling:
  - ≥4 episodes/year; associated with worse prognosis

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
DIAGNOSIS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  - DSM-5-TR diagnostic criteria: BD-I, BD-II, Cyclothymic Disorder,
    Other Specified BD
  - Clinical interview; mood charting/life charts
  - Mood Disorder Questionnaire (MDQ): Screening for BD
  - Young Mania Rating Scale (YMRS); Hamilton/Montgomery–Åsberg Depression
    Rating Scale
  - Exclude medical causes: Thyroid disease (TSH), B12 deficiency, CBC,
    CMP, neurological evaluation
  - EEG (if seizure disorder suspected), brain MRI (if first episode/atypical)
  - Substance use assessment (commonly comorbid)

Differential Diagnosis:
  - Unipolar major depressive disorder
  - Schizophrenia/schizoaffective disorder
  - ADHD, borderline personality disorder
  - Substance-induced mood disorder
  - Medical causes: Thyroid disease, Cushing's, epilepsy

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
TREATMENT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

1. MOOD STABILIZERS — Cornerstone of BD Management

   Lithium (Eskalith, Lithobid):
     - Mechanism: Inhibits inositol monophosphatase (IP), GSK-3β; modulates
       serotonin, dopamine, neuroprotective effects
     - Dose: 600–1800 mg/day in divided doses (IR) or once daily (ER)
     - Target serum level: Acute mania: 0.8–1.2 mEq/L;
       Maintenance: 0.6–0.8 mEq/L
     - Gold standard for mania prevention, depression prevention, and
       suicide risk reduction (ONLY mood stabilizer with antisuicide evidence)
     - Monitoring: Serum lithium level (5–7 days after dose change, then
       every 3–6 months); renal function (eGFR, creatinine); thyroid function
       (TSH, T4); ECG if cardiac history
     - Side effects: Fine tremor, polyuria/polydipsia (nephrogenic DI),
       hypothyroidism, weight gain, acne, cognitive dulling
     - Toxicity (>1.5 mEq/L): Coarse tremor, ataxia, confusion, seizures
     - Narrow therapeutic index; avoid NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors, thiazides
       (increase lithium levels)

   Valproic Acid / Divalproex (Depakote, Depakene):
     - Mechanism: Increases GABA activity; inhibits voltage-gated sodium
       channels; histone deacetylase (HDAC) inhibition
     - Dose: 750–2000 mg/day; target serum level 50–125 mcg/mL
     - Extended-release (Depakote ER) for improved tolerability
     - Particularly effective for: Mixed states, rapid cycling, mania
     - Side effects: Weight gain, tremor, alopecia, GI upset, sedation,
       polycystic ovarian syndrome (PCOS)
     - Serious: Hepatotoxicity (rare), pancreatitis, teratogenicity
       (neural tube defects — avoid in women of childbearing age)
     - Monitoring: LFTs, CBC (thrombocytopenia), drug levels, valproate level

   Lamotrigine (Lamictal):
     - Mechanism: Inhibits voltage-gated sodium and calcium channels; reduces
       glutamate release
     - Dose: Slow titration required — 25 mg/day × 2 weeks, 50 mg/day ×
       2 weeks, target 100–200 mg/day (BD-I: up to 400 mg)
     - DIFFERENT dosing with valproate (halve doses) and enzyme inducers
       (double doses)
     - Most effective for bipolar depression and maintenance (preventing
       depressive episodes); less effective for acute mania
     - First-line maintenance, especially BD-II
     - Risk: Stevens-Johnson syndrome (SJS) / toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN)
       — rare but serious; linked to rapid titration
     - Side effects: Headache, dizziness, diplopia, blurred vision
     - Monitoring: Clinical monitoring for rash; no blood level monitoring
       in standard practice

   Carbamazepine (Tegretol):
     - 400–1600 mg/day in divided doses; therapeutic level 4–12 mcg/mL
     - Mechanism: Voltage-gated sodium channel blocker
     - Effective for acute mania and mixed states; less evidence for
       bipolar depression
     - Significant drug interactions (P450 inducer)
     - Risk: Aplastic anemia, agranulocytosis (rare), SJS
     - Monitoring: CBC, LFTs, sodium, carbamazepine level
     - HLA-B*1502 testing required before use in Asian populations (SJS risk)

   Oxcarbazepine: 300–2400 mg/day; less drug interactions than carbamazepine;
   less evidence base

2. ATYPICAL ANTIPSYCHOTICS (for acute mania and maintenance)

   Quetiapine (Seroquel):
     - 300–800 mg/day at bedtime
     - FDA-approved for acute mania, bipolar depression (Seroquel XR 300 mg),
       and maintenance
     - Dopamine D2, serotonin 5-HT2A antagonism
     - Side effects: Sedation, weight gain, metabolic syndrome, orthostatic
       hypotension
     - Most evidence for bipolar depression among antipsychotics

   Olanzapine (Zyprexa):
     - 5–20 mg/day; highly effective for acute mania
     - Olanzapine-fluoxetine combination (Symbyax): Approved for bipolar
       depression (6/25 or 12/50 mg)
     - High risk of metabolic complications (weight gain, diabetes, dyslipidemia)

   Aripiprazole (Abilify):
     - 15–30 mg/day (oral) or 400 mg/month (IM depot)
     - Dopamine partial agonist; weight-neutral; approved for mania and
       maintenance
     - Side effects: Akathisia, insomnia, nausea

   Risperidone (Risperdal):
     - 1–6 mg/day; approved for acute mania
     - Risperidone LAI (Risperdal Consta): 25–50 mg IM every 2 weeks

   Lurasidone (Latuda):
     - 20–120 mg/day with food (≥350 calories)
     - FDA-approved for bipolar depression (monotherapy and adjunct to
       lithium/valproate)
     - Minimal metabolic effects; weight-neutral

   Cariprazine (Vraylar):
     - 1.5–6 mg/day; dopamine D3/D2 partial agonist
     - FDA-approved for acute mania/mixed features AND bipolar depression
     - Metabolically favorable

   Asenapine (Saphris):
     - 10–20 mg/day sublingual; approved for acute mania and mixed features

   Ziprasidone (Geodon):
     - 40–160 mg/day; QTc monitoring required

3. ACUTE MANIA TREATMENT
   - First-line: Lithium OR valproate OR atypical antipsychotic (quetiapine,
     risperidone, olanzapine, aripiprazole, cariprazine, asenapine)
   - Combination (lithium or valproate + antipsychotic) for severe mania
   - Benzodiazepines (lorazepam 1–2 mg IV/IM, clonazepam 0.5–2 mg):
     Short-term adjunct for agitation and sleep in acute phase
   - ECT: Rapid, highly effective for severe/refractory mania

4. BIPOLAR DEPRESSION TREATMENT
   - First-line: Quetiapine (300 mg/day) OR Lithium + lamotrigine;
     Lurasidone (as monotherapy or adjunct); Cariprazine 1.5 mg/day
   - Olanzapine-fluoxetine combination (OFC/Symbyax)
   - Lithium (monotherapy or augmentation)
   - Lamotrigine (maintenance/prevention; less evidence for acute bipolar depression)
   - Antidepressants: CONTROVERSIAL — risk of inducing mania/mixed states/
     rapid cycling; may be considered with mood stabilizer cover; avoid
     tricyclic antidepressants (higher switch risk); use shortest duration
   - ECT: Effective for severe bipolar depression with psychosis, catatonia,
     or suicidality

   Lumateperone (Caplyta):
     - 42 mg/day; FDA-approved for bipolar depression (BD-I and BD-II)
     - Serotonin/dopamine modulator; minimal metabolic effects

5. MAINTENANCE THERAPY
   - Goal: Prevent recurrence of manic and depressive episodes; protect
     neurocognitive function; reduce suicidality
   - First-line: Lithium; quetiapine; lamotrigine (depressive polarity);
     valproate; aripiprazole (depot)
   - Combination therapy often needed for breakthrough episodes

6. RAPID CYCLING AND MIXED STATES
   - Valproate or lithium combination; add atypical antipsychotic
   - Avoid antidepressants; reduce triggers (sleep deprivation, substances)
   - Thyroid optimization (T4 supplementation if subclinical hypothyroidism)

7. PSYCHOSOCIAL INTERVENTIONS
   - Psychoeducation: Foundation of BD self-management; improves adherence
   - Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT): Addresses negative thought patterns
   - Interpersonal and Social Rhythm Therapy (IPSRT): Stabilizes daily routines
     and circadian rhythms
   - Family-Focused Therapy (FFT): Reduces expressed emotion; improves family
     functioning
   - Group therapy, peer support

8. SUICIDE PREVENTION
   - Highest-risk period: Depressive or mixed phases; after hospitalization
   - Lithium: Reduces suicide risk by ~50–80% in BD; only agent with
     anti-suicidal evidence
   - Safety planning; lethal means restriction; crisis line access
   - Hospitalization: For active suicidal ideation with plan/intent

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
MONITORING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  - Lithium serum levels, renal function, thyroid function every 6 months
  - Valproate levels, LFTs, CBC: Every 6 months
  - Metabolic monitoring (weight, BMI, glucose, lipids): Annually (especially
    on atypical antipsychotics)
  - Mood charting (mood diary, apps): Daily tracking
  - Clinical assessment for emerging episodes: Every 3–6 months

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
COMPLICATIONS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
  - Suicide (15–20× higher risk than general population)
  - Substance use disorders (>50% comorbidity)
  - Cardiovascular disease (major cause of premature mortality)
  - Neurocognitive impairment (residual between episodes)
  - Social and occupational dysfunction
  - Medication side effects: Lithium nephropathy, thyroid disease;
    metabolic syndrome with antipsychotics
  - Comorbid anxiety disorders (~50%), ADHD
