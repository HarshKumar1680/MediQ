Title: Pneumonia - Treatment and Medical Management
Source: Based on Wikipedia, Mayo Clinic, Infectious Diseases Society of America
Disease: Pneumonia

Pneumonia is an inflammatory condition of the lung parenchyma primarily affecting the alveoli. It is most commonly caused by bacterial, viral, or fungal infections. Pneumonia is a leading cause of morbidity and mortality globally, particularly in children under five and elderly adults.

OVERVIEW
Pneumonia is classified by the causative agent (bacterial, viral, fungal), by acquisition setting (community-acquired, hospital-acquired, ventilator-associated), and by pattern of lung involvement (lobar, bronchopneumonia, interstitial).

COMMON CAUSATIVE AGENTS
Streptococcus pneumoniae (most common bacterial cause of community-acquired pneumonia), Haemophilus influenzae, Mycoplasma pneumoniae, Legionella pneumophila, Staphylococcus aureus, respiratory viruses including influenza, RSV, and SARS-CoV-2, and fungi such as Pneumocystis jirovecii in immunocompromised patients.

SYMPTOMS
Symptoms include fever, chills, cough with purulent sputum, pleuritic chest pain, shortness of breath, rapid breathing, and fatigue. Atypical pneumonia may present with a more gradual onset and milder symptoms.

DIAGNOSIS
Diagnosis is based on clinical presentation, chest X-ray showing infiltrates or consolidation, complete blood count, sputum culture, blood culture, urinary antigen tests for Legionella and pneumococcus, and oxygen saturation measurement.

TREATMENT APPROACHES

Community-Acquired Pneumonia (CAP):
For outpatient treatment in previously healthy adults: amoxicillin 500mg three times daily, or a macrolide (azithromycin or clarithromycin), or doxycycline.
For outpatient treatment with comorbidities: respiratory fluoroquinolone (levofloxacin or moxifloxacin) or beta-lactam plus macrolide combination.
For hospitalized non-ICU patients: beta-lactam (ampicillin-sulbactam, cefotaxime, ceftriaxone) plus macrolide, or respiratory fluoroquinolone alone.
For severe pneumonia requiring ICU admission: beta-lactam plus azithromycin, or beta-lactam plus fluoroquinolone. Anti-MRSA coverage (vancomycin or linezolid) added if MRSA is suspected.

Hospital-Acquired Pneumonia (HAP) / Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia (VAP):
Empirical broad-spectrum antibiotics covering gram-negative bacilli (including Pseudomonas) and MRSA are initiated. De-escalation based on culture results is essential.
Typical agents include piperacillin-tazobactam, cefepime, meropenem, or imipenem, combined with vancomycin or linezolid.

Viral Pneumonia:
Influenza pneumonia is treated with oseltamivir within 48 hours of symptom onset. Supportive care including oxygen supplementation, hydration, and antipyretics is the mainstay of viral pneumonia treatment.

Fungal Pneumonia:
Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia (PCP) is treated with trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole (co-trimoxazole). Aspergillus pneumonia is treated with voriconazole.

Supportive Care:
Supplemental oxygen for hypoxia, intravenous fluids for dehydration, bronchodilators for wheeze, analgesics for pleuritic pain, and mechanical ventilation for respiratory failure constitute supportive management.

SEVERITY ASSESSMENT
The CURB-65 score (Confusion, Urea, Respiratory rate, Blood pressure, age 65+) guides the decision between outpatient, inpatient, and ICU admission.

PREVENTION
Pneumococcal vaccines (PCV13 and PPSV23) and annual influenza vaccination significantly reduce pneumonia risk in high-risk populations.
